# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 AI Decision Support in Safety-Critical Systems

Across automotive, avionics, railway, industrial robotics, and maritime domains, AI has moved beyond simple automation into a core advisory role, providing predictions, risk assessments, and operational recommendations at the point of decision (Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md)). Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), in a systematic review of 91 studies on collaborative intelligence, identify two primary interaction modes: machine-assists-human and human-assists-machine. In both, AI functions as an advisory layer. Ramos et al. (2024) find this pattern consistent across all six industries reviewed: AI provides assessments and predictions, but humans retain final decision authority because the consequences of error in safety-critical contexts cannot be transferred to a machine. The International AI Safety Report notes that general-purpose AI capabilities are expanding rapidly, with systems now performing reasoning, planning, and autonomous action at scales not anticipated even two years prior (Bengio et al., 2026 [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md)). As a result, AI advisory systems are expanding into more safety-critical domains, increasing the consequences of AI behaviour going wrong.

These capabilities are well evidenced. AI fuses heterogeneous inputs, including sensor readings, forecasts, and historical records, at latencies unattainable by human operators. This reduces cognitive load and maintains situational awareness under time pressure (Ramos et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md)). Bloomfield and Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) go further: AI enables real-time perception and dynamic risk estimation that conventional software cannot replicate. Bengio, Hinton et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Managing%20extreme%20AI%20risks%20amid%20rapid%20progress.md), in a consensus statement by 25 leading AI researchers, document benefits across scientific research, healthcare, and engineering.

AI systems also produce incorrect outputs, and in safety-critical environments, incorrect recommendations do not simply degrade performance. They anchor human decisions toward unsafe outcomes. Wen et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Risk%20Perception%20in%20Complex%20Systems-%20A%20Comparative%20Analysis%20of%20Process%20Control%20and%20Autonomous%20Vehicle%20Failures.md) demonstrate this across 60 real-world accident reports: human intervention was ineffective in 83.3% of process control cases and 66.7% of autonomous vehicle cases. The dominant failure mode was not technical malfunction but human–automation interaction breakdown: over-reliance, mode confusion, passive monitoring. Risk arises not only from AI outputs but from how humans respond to them. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) identify the same gap from the formal methods side: across 46 studies, nearly all safety enforcement is applied reactively after AI produces output, rather than restricting what AI is permitted to produce. Bloomfield and Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) state the implication directly — AI and ML components are opaque, experimentally derived, and cannot satisfy classical fault-avoidance requirements. Systems must account for both correct outputs and failure cases. Verification alone does not answer a more fundamental question: the conditions under which AI should be allowed to generate any output in the first place.

Current research addresses this challenge primarily through two strategies: improving AI accuracy and improving AI explainability. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), surveying safety techniques across industrial and transportation domains, identify safety bags, safety monitors, and safety envelopes as the dominant runtime mechanisms. All three verify or filter AI outputs after generation. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) confirm this pattern across a broader scope: they survey eight categories of formal methods (reachability, SMT verification, model checking, runtime verification, shielding, control barrier functions, risk verification, and MILP/ILP) and find that none governs AI participation or advisory scope as a function of classified operational conditions. The field has focused on making AI outputs safer or more transparent. The literature leaves both questions unasked: in which environmental conditions AI should be allowed to participate, and what types of recommendations it may offer when it does. The governance of AI behaviour under varying safety conditions, not just the correctness of AI outputs, is the central concern of this review. This chapter examines how existing systems control AI behaviour under risk. It organises that examination around two governance levels. Level 1 concerns whether AI may participate. Level 2 concerns what AI may recommend. The central finding is that while Level 1 has matured considerably across the literature, Level 2 remains absent. Existing architectures address one level or the other; the two have not been unified under a shared environmental state conditioning.

## 2.2 Deterministic Safety Constraints and Safety Gating

Across the reviewed literature, one architectural response appears consistently: deterministic safety constraints that enforce properties the AI cannot guarantee on its own. These mechanisms operate alongside or around the AI system rather than within it. Four patterns dominate. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) formalise *shields*, runtime mechanisms synthesised from LTL safety specifications and environment models that intercept unsafe actions before they reach the environment. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) propose Guaranteed Safe AI, a framework built on three components: a world model, a safety specification, and a verifier that produces auditable proof certificates before AI output is deployed. Bajcsy and Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) take a control-theoretic approach, defining a Human-AI Safety Filter that monitors AI outputs and intervenes at the last possible moment to prevent trajectory-level safety violations. Hamel-De le Court, Belardinelli and Goodall (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Probabilistic%20Shielding%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) extend the shielding paradigm to stochastic environments, constructing a probabilistic shield with two formal guarantees: Theorem 1 proves the shielded policy satisfies safety constraints with probability at least 1 − δ, and Theorem 2 proves convergence of the underlying learning algorithm is preserved. Despite the probabilistic formulation, the shield mechanism remains binary — it intervenes when estimated risk exceeds a threshold, and permits the action otherwise. All four share a structural principle: a deterministic layer constrains a probabilistic AI layer. The AI cannot be trusted to self-regulate.

In all four patterns, the mechanism is the same: a binary gate. Whether implemented as an LTL-based shield, an auditable proof certificate, a control-theoretic filter, or a probabilistic threshold, each system either permits the AI output or replaces it with a safe alternative (Könighofer et al., 2025 [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md); Dalrymple et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md); Bajcsy & Fisac, 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md)). More recent work refines this pattern but does not change its binary character. Corsi et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Verification-Guided%20Shielding%20for%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) use formal DNN verification to partition the input space into provably safe and potentially unsafe regions, activating the shield only where needed. This reduces overhead by 25–71%. However, the shield itself still operates as a binary allow-or-replace gate. Abella et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SAFEXPLAIN-%20a%20Complete%20Approach%20Towards%20Trustworthy%20AI-Based%20Safety-Critical%20Systems.md), in the EU SAFEXPLAIN project, implement a supervision function that bounds AI operations within a predefined safety envelope and can switch to a non-AI fallback subsystem. Vermaelen and Holvoet (2025) [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md) use constraint-based planning with state rules that exclude any action whose foreseeable outcomes violate safety conditions. Banerjee et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/CRANE-%20Reasoning%20with%20Constrained%20LLM%20Generation.md), in CRANE, implement a structurally different variant: rather than intercepting outputs post-hoc, they constrain an LLM's output tokens during generation by intersecting the model's output grammar with a safety grammar, restricting the set of admissible outputs at the decoding level. The constraint is still binary: the model alternates between unconstrained reasoning segments and grammar-constrained output segments, with no intermediate restriction mode. Every examined system makes a binary governance decision. None implements an intermediate mode.

This binary pattern is not an artefact of a few selected papers. Four independent systematic reviews confirm it from different bodies of literature: Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) find no system across 91 collaborative intelligence studies that varies advisory scope by classified safety state; Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) identify shielding, runtime verification, and control barrier functions as the dominant enforcement mechanisms across 46 formal methods studies — all binary; Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) confirm that risk tiering across 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks triggers binary deployment decisions with no graduated advisory scope; and Odriozola-Olalde et al. (2023) [[notes]](../notes/Shielded%20Reinforcement%20Learning-%20A%20review%20of%20reactive%20methods%20for%20safe%20learning.md), in a dedicated review of shielded reinforcement learning, classify methods into three Safety Levels, but all three implement binary per-action gates. Restricting the *types* of actions an agent may propose as a function of environmental state remains outside the scope of every reviewed method. Zhang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Developing%20real-time%20IoT-based%20public%20safety%20alert%20and%20emergency%20response%20systems.md), developing a real-time IoT-based public safety system, provide the most explicit formalisation of this binary ceiling: their alert function φ(X(t)) = {1 if P_alert(X(t)) ≥ θ; 0 otherwise} (Equation 4) maps environmental sensor readings to a hard binary decision with no intermediate response, despite the system having continuous access to environmental state data. Banerjee et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/CRANE-%20Reasoning%20with%20Constrained%20LLM%20Generation.md) provide the strongest theoretical motivation for why binary restriction is suboptimal. Their Proposition 3.1 proves via circuit complexity that constraining an LLM's output to conform to a grammar G during reasoning reduces the model's expressivity to TC⁰, the complexity class of constant-depth threshold circuits, meaning the model loses the ability to perform multi-step reasoning while its output is constrained. Their solution, the augmented grammar G_a = R_M·G, restores full expressivity by alternating unconstrained reasoning with constrained output. This is a formal proof that binary output constraint degrades capability, and that an intermediate mode — constrained output within unconstrained reasoning — preserves it. The absence of an intermediate governance mode is consistent and cross-domain.

Whether AI should act at all is an important question, and these safety constraints answer it. But a second question remains unasked, let alone answered: conditional on acting, what should AI be permitted to recommend? Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) classify AI safety compliance into three classes, where Class II systems use a certified safety bag to monitor an uncertified AI item. Even in this most structured pattern, the safety bag checks whether AI output is safe. It does not restrict the scope of what the AI is permitted to produce in the first place. In the terminology of this review, existing safety constraints implement Level 1 governance, which controls whether AI participates in decision-making. Advisory scope, what the AI is permitted to recommend once the gate is open, remains ungoverned. If the gate is open, the AI operates at full capability regardless of risk conditions. In practice, this means the same range of recommendations (timing optimisations, route suggestions, duration estimates) would be presented whether conditions are calm or deteriorating. This confirms that a binary governance model is insufficient. An intermediate mode is required — one in which AI participates within a restricted advisory scope calibrated to the severity of conditions. This leaves a second unresolved question: if safety constraints govern participation, do authority allocation frameworks govern what AI may recommend?

## 2.3 Human–AI Decision Authority

Safety constraints determine whether AI acts. A different question, who holds decision authority once AI is permitted to act, has generated its own research tradition. The central idea is that safety depends not only on constraining AI actions but on defining the respective roles of human and machine in the decision process. Several classification frameworks have been proposed. Porter et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/INSYTE-%20A%20Classification%20Framework%20for%20Traditional%20to%20Agentic%20AI%20Systems.md) develop INSYTE, an eight-dimension framework that classifies AI systems across a spectrum from traditional rule-based tools to fully agentic systems. Two of its dimensions, independence from intervention and independence from oversight, directly characterise how much human governance exists over AI behaviour. Bach, Kristiansen et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Unpacking%20Human-AI%20Interaction%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Systematic%20Literature%20Review.md), reviewing 24 empirical studies of human-AI interaction in safety-critical industries, identify four primary roles for AI: collaborative AI, AI-assisted decision-making, human-controlled AI, and emotional AI. AI-assisted decision-making, where the AI advises and the human retains final authority, dominates, appearing in 16 of 24 studies. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) organise their 91-paper review around the same distinction: machine assists human, or human assists machine. Feng, McDonald and Zhang (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Levels%20of%20Autonomy%20for%20AI%20Agents.md) take a different approach, decomposing governance into two explicit dimensions: *agency* (what tools and resources the AI agent can access) and *autonomy* (how much human involvement is required in its operation). They propose five autonomy levels (L1 through L5) and introduce the concept of autonomy certificates: formal credentials that certify an agent's eligibility for a given autonomy level based on verified capabilities. This is the most conceptually granular framework in the reviewed literature because it names two governance dimensions rather than one. Across all these frameworks, the organising question is *who decides* (the human, the AI, or some combination) and, in Feng et al.'s case, *with what tools and under what oversight*.

The mechanism for managing this authority varies. In some systems, authority is allocated at design time and remains fixed during operation. In others, it shifts dynamically based on task context. Saup et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/From%20pilots%20to%20decision%20systems-%20embedding%20generative%20AI%20into%20strategic%20decision-making%20through%20a%20socio-technical%20and%20governance%20lens.md) propose four admission gates (quality, provenance, explainability, and accountability) that determine whether AI outputs are admitted into human strategic deliberation. These gates are organisational checkpoints: they filter AI outputs *after* generation, based on whether the output meets governance criteria. Madsen and Kim (2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md), reviewing AI decision transparency for autonomous shipping, find that the dominant approach is transparency-based: colour-coded risk displays, route visualisations, and layered information interfaces that help operators understand AI reasoning. The implicit assumption, that comprehension enables safe override, is refuted by Tatasciore and Loft (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Calibrating%20Reliance%20on%20Automated%20Advice-%20Transparency%20and%20Trust%20Calibration%20Feedback.md): telling participants their trust was miscalibrated produced zero measurable effect on reliance behaviour. Comprehension and safe override are not the same thing. Leppinen et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/A%20Stage-Gate%20Decision%20Process%20for%20Guiding%20the%20Development%20of%20AI%20Solutions%20for%20Preventive%20Maintenance.md) apply a stage-gate process with go/no-go decisions, but at the development level, governing which AI solutions proceed through the pipeline, not what a deployed AI system is permitted to output at runtime.

These frameworks address an important dimension of AI safety, who decides, but they do not constrain the range of outputs the AI can generate. Porter et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/INSYTE-%20A%20Classification%20Framework%20for%20Traditional%20to%20Agentic%20AI%20Systems.md) classify intervention and oversight independence on a scale, but this scale describes how much a human is involved, not what types of recommendations the AI is allowed to generate. A system rated at INSYTE Level 3 (AI makes decisions with human oversight) still produces the same outputs regardless of operational risk. Bach, Kristiansen et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Unpacking%20Human-AI%20Interaction%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Systematic%20Literature%20Review.md) find no system in their review that restricts AI output scope based on classified safety state — one study notes that users relied on AI more in low-risk than high-risk conditions, but this is informal behavioural adaptation, not architectural governance. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) confirm this across a larger corpus: authority allocation frameworks do not vary AI advisory scope. Feng et al.'s (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Levels%20of%20Autonomy%20for%20AI%20Agents.md) framework reveals the limitation most precisely. Although it identifies two governance dimensions (agency and autonomy), both are configured at design time. An agent certified at autonomy Level 3 retains that level regardless of whether operational conditions are safe or dangerous. The framework also treats environment as a fixed deployment context (e.g., "healthcare setting" or "financial domain"), not as a variable that changes during operation. Most critically, it is missing a third lever: restricting the *scope* of what the AI may recommend. Agency controls tool access; autonomy controls human involvement. Neither governs the range of advisory outputs the AI is permitted to generate. The AI produces the same recommendations whether conditions are safe or dangerous.

The gap reveals that authority allocation and advisory scope governance are orthogonal problems. Authority allocation answers who decides. It is structurally silent on what AI may recommend. Knowing the human has final decision authority does not remove unreliable AI recommendations from the display. Madsen and Kim (2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md) confirm this directly: autonomous shipping systems use colour-coded risk levels to vary what operators *see*, but do not vary what the AI is permitted to *generate*. The governance question in that literature is "can the human understand and override the AI?" — not "what should the AI be permitted to recommend given current conditions?" These are different questions. These frameworks resolve the participation question. The advisory scope question, what AI may recommend under different conditions, falls outside their design. If static authority allocation cannot answer the second question, the natural step is systems that adapt AI behaviour based on risk at runtime.

## 2.4 Adaptive Autonomy and Risk-Based Systems

Static authority allocation has clear limits. A growing body of work addresses them through adaptation: systems that change their behaviour when risk conditions shift by increasing monitoring, reducing autonomy, or switching to a safer mode entirely. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) propose a traffic-light degradation index for AI in safety-critical systems, classifying AI performance into three levels: green (nominal), orange (degradation detected), and red (threshold exceeded). At red, the system switches to a conventional non-AI backup controller. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md), reviewing safety evidence methods for automated driving, identify a similar hierarchy: Operational Design Domain (ODD), Restricted Operational Domain (ROD), and Minimal Risk Condition (MRC). The system operates nominally within its ODD, degrades to a restricted domain when capabilities are reduced, and transitions to a minimal risk state as a last resort. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) formalises a K-tier graduated permission architecture for AI economic agents, where a gate function maps verified robustness scores to discrete permission tiers — each tier defining a different set of permitted actions. All three approaches share the principle that governance should be graduated rather than binary.

The mechanisms differ in detail but follow a common pattern: classify a condition, then adjust system behaviour accordingly. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) compute the traffic-light index from performance metrics, outlier detection, and concept drift monitoring, all non-AI statistical methods applied to the AI's own behaviour. At Level 2 (orange), the supervisory component conducts thorough checks and investigates root causes. At Level 3 (red), it switches to the backup. Structured around a weakest-link principle, Baxi's (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) architecture determines each agent's tier by its worst robustness dimension, with a formal proof that economic exposure is bounded by the verified tier. Li et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Safety-Enhanced%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning%20for%20Autonomous%20Driving-%20Dare%20to%20Make%20Mistakes%20to%20Learn%20Better%20and%20Faster.md), working in autonomous driving, apply a dynamic risk threshold that evaluates anticipated risk per action and replaces unsafe actions with safe alternatives, reducing collision rates by over 92%. Graduated, risk-responsive governance is technically feasible. Kwon et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Runtime%20Safety%20through%20Adaptive%20Shielding-%20From%20Hidden%20Parameter%20Inference%20to%20Provable%20Guarantees.md) take adaptation further than any system reviewed here. Their adaptive shielding framework incorporates environment-centric features E ∈ ℝ^n₂ (observable properties of the operating environment) into a SafetyScore that drives runtime shield adjustment via an Adaptive Constraint Parameter (ACP) threshold Γ_t. Crucially, the ACP updates at runtime as the agent gathers information about hidden environmental parameters, and Theorem 1 provides a formal safety guarantee that the shielded policy satisfies constraints throughout this adaptation. This makes Kwon et al. the only reviewed system that combines environment-as-governance-input with runtime adaptation and a formal safety proof. Banerjee et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/CRANE-%20Reasoning%20with%20Constrained%20LLM%20Generation.md) provide a structural precursor from the NLP domain: their augmented grammar G_a = R_M·G defines an output set containment relationship (constrained outputs are a strict subset of unconstrained outputs) that is formally analogous to A_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A_AI(SAFE). However, the alternation between constrained and unconstrained modes is syntactically triggered by delimiter tokens, not conditioned on classified environmental state.

The limitation, however, is consistent across all these systems. What changes between levels is monitoring intensity, control authority, or individual action correction. The scope of what the AI is permitted to recommend does not change. In Flehmig et al.'s (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) traffic-light framework, the AI produces the same types of outputs at Level 1 (green) and Level 2 (orange). The difference is purely supervisory: routine checks versus thorough investigation. The AI's recommendation space does not shrink. Baxi's (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) architecture comes closest to advisory scope restriction because permission sets genuinely vary by tier, but the classification is based on the agent's own verified robustness, not on environmental conditions. A highly robust agent receives broad permissions regardless of whether it operates in a safe or dangerous environment. Li et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Safety-Enhanced%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning%20for%20Autonomous%20Driving-%20Dare%20to%20Make%20Mistakes%20to%20Learn%20Better%20and%20Faster.md) correct individual unsafe actions but never restrict the *types* of actions the agent may propose. The agent always generates full-scope outputs; only specific violations are intercepted. Kwon et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Runtime%20Safety%20through%20Adaptive%20Shielding-%20From%20Hidden%20Parameter%20Inference%20to%20Provable%20Guarantees.md) come closer than any other reviewed system. They use environmental features as governance input, adapt at runtime, and provide formal safety guarantees. However, their SafetyScore remains binary. Each state-action pair is classified as safe or unsafe, with no intermediate category. The adaptation is quantitative — the ACP threshold Γ_t shifts along a continuous scale — not qualitative. No discrete governance modes analogous to SAFE, CAUTION, and UNSAFE emerge from this mechanism. Kwon et al. adapt *how strictly* the shield intervenes. They do not adapt *what the AI is permitted to recommend*.

This clarifies the architectural gap. Existing graduated systems adapt *how the system is supervised* or *how individual actions are checked*, but none formally restricts *what the AI is allowed to recommend* as a function of classified environmental state. Flehmig et al.'s (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) traffic-light model is the closest structural analogue (three classified levels, non-AI classification, different responses per level), but the intermediate level governs the *supervisor*, not the *AI*. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) provide the formal machinery for constraining AI at runtime through shields, but those shields operate per-action, not per-state. The literature supplies each governance dimension separately; unifying participation governance with advisory scope governance under the same classified environmental state is the gap these systems do not fill. Governance intensity adapts. The range of what AI may recommend does not. The guardrails literature approaches this output restriction question directly, though through post-hoc filtering rather than pre-hoc state classification.

## 2.5 AI Governance and Guardrails

If safety constraints control participation and authority frameworks define roles, a third approach targets AI outputs directly: guardrails — runtime mechanisms that intercept, evaluate, and filter AI outputs to prevent unsafe or undesirable behaviour. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md), drawing on the Swiss Cheese Model from accident theory, propose a multi-layered guardrail architecture for foundation model agents, where each layer addresses a different quality attribute across different pipeline stages: prompts, intermediate results, and final outputs. Their taxonomy identifies 13 distinct guardrail actions, including block, filter, flag, modify, validate, and human intervention. Chen et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20Safety%20Landscape%20for%20Large%20Language%20Models-%20Taxonomy%2C%20State-of-the-art%2C%20and%20Future%20Directions.md) formalise the guardrail system as an AI pipeline (an input filtering module, the LLM, and an output filtering module) and define two AI Safety Principles: output constraint (the output space must be disjoint from prohibited sets) and runtime constraint (the system must operate within predefined requirements). Wang et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/AgentSpec-%20Customizable%20Runtime%20Enforcement%20for%20Safe%20and%20Reliable%20LLM%20Agents.md) develop AgentSpec, a domain-specific language for runtime enforcement that allows developers to define structured rules composed of triggers, predicates, and enforcement actions, preventing over 90% of unsafe code executions in their evaluation. The guardrail literature is technically mature and offers a rich vocabulary for describing output governance.

Guardrails work as post-hoc filters: the AI generates an output, and the guardrail evaluates it against predefined rules before it reaches the user or the environment. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) make this explicit: guardrails are "mechanisms integrated into an agent's architecture to safeguard its behavior during runtime." Chen, Kang and Li (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md), in SHIELDAGENT, push this further by extracting LTL rules from policy documents and using formal verification to evaluate each agent action against the relevant rule circuits. The result is a binary safety label per action, safe or unsafe, with violated rules and explanations. Liang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md), applying the Guaranteed Safe AI framework to semantic communication networks, implement a gatekeeper that filters AI outputs falling below a quality threshold, achieving a 54% improvement in output accuracy. The OWASP Agentic AI Top 10 (2025) [[notes]](../notes/OWASP%20Top%2010%20for%20Agentic%20Applications%202026.md) confirms this pattern at industry scale. The document identifies ten threat categories for agentic AI systems (goal hijacking, tool misuse, privilege abuse, supply chain compromise, remote code execution, memory poisoning, inter-agent communication failures, cascading failures, human-trust exploitation, and rogue agents) and catalogues hundreds of mitigations across them. Every mitigation operates as a binary gate: permit or block, allow or deny, approve or reject, execute or quarantine. The closest entry to graduated governance is ASI09, "Adaptive Trust Calibration," which recommends adjusting human oversight intensity based on contextual risk. But this adjusts how closely the human watches. What the AI may recommend stays the same. In each case, the guardrail intercepts what the AI has already produced. What the AI attempts to produce remains unchanged.

This post-hoc architecture has a structural consequence. Because guardrails evaluate outputs after generation, they apply the same filtering rules regardless of environmental conditions. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) acknowledge "context-dependent rules" as one of four rule types, but context here refers to data or deployment context (user location, regulatory jurisdiction), not classified environmental safety state. The guardrail configuration does not change when conditions become dangerous. Chen et al.'s (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20Safety%20Landscape%20for%20Large%20Language%20Models-%20Taxonomy%2C%20State-of-the-art%2C%20and%20Future%20Directions.md) AI Safety Principle I formalises this as a static constraint: the output space must be disjoint from prohibited sets. The prohibited set does not vary with operational state. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), assessing the state of risk management across 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks, conclude that "no combination of methods ensures the high reliability required in critical domains." The OWASP Agentic AI Top 10 (2025) [[notes]](../notes/OWASP%20Top%2010%20for%20Agentic%20Applications%202026.md) reaches the same conclusion from industry practice: across ten threat categories and hundreds of mitigations reflecting global consensus among security practitioners, no mitigation conditions AI scope on environmental state. The binary framing is not merely an academic default — it is the operational standard adopted by the largest industry body addressing agentic AI security. Part of the reason is that both academic and industry approaches treat safety as a fixed property of the AI system rather than a dynamic function of the operating environment.

The distinction between post-hoc filtering and pre-hoc output space definition is central to this review. Guardrails ask: "Is this specific output acceptable?" The question this review poses is different: "What *types* of outputs should the AI be permitted to generate, given current conditions?" Wang et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/AgentSpec-%20Customizable%20Runtime%20Enforcement%20for%20Safe%20and%20Reliable%20LLM%20Agents.md) illustrate the difference clearly. AgentSpec evaluates each planned action against applicable rules, but the agent's action space remains the same regardless of environmental state. Unsafe actions are caught individually. There is no mechanism that narrows the admissible output space *before* the AI reasons, as a function of classified safety conditions. Abella et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SAFEXPLAIN-%20a%20Complete%20Approach%20Towards%20Trustworthy%20AI-Based%20Safety-Critical%20Systems.md) come closer with a supervision function that constrains AI operations within a predefined safety envelope, but whether the envelope varies by classified state is not specified or formalised. A governance mechanism that defines the AI's permitted output space before generation — and varies that space as a function of classified environmental safety state — does not appear in the reviewed literature. Governance defines what AI should be permitted to do. The mechanism by which AI combines deterministic rules with probabilistic inference determines what is technically possible to govern.

## 2.6 Hybrid AI Systems

Hybrid AI systems combine rule-based and probabilistic components to address the limitations of purely statistical models in safety-critical domains. The most direct architectural response is strict role separation. Castagnone and Nitti (2026) [[notes]](../notes/A%20Neuro-Symbolic%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Deterministic%20Reliability%20in%20AI-Assisted%20Structural%20Engineering-%20The%20SYNAPSE%20Architecture.md) demonstrate this in the SYNAPSE architecture: a neural component handles natural language understanding and result presentation, while a deterministic symbolic engine performs all safety-critical calculations. The design principle is unambiguous: the neural component is never permitted to compute safety-critical outputs directly. Punzi et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/AI%2C%20Meet%20Human-%20Learning%20Paradigms%20for%20Hybrid%20Decision-Making%20Systems.md) survey this integration more broadly, identifying three paradigms of increasing human-machine coupling: human oversight (accepting or rejecting machine predictions), learning to abstain (the machine learns when to defer), and bidirectional collaboration where human and machine co-learn. In emergency medicine, Bhuvaneswari et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/A%20human-centered%20hybrid%20AI%20framework%20for%20optimizing%20emergency%20triage%20in%20resource-constrained%20settings.md) deploy a similar hybrid logic in resource-constrained settings: a rule-based cognitive module encodes clinical heuristics, a lightweight CNN handles risk prediction, a resource-aware optimiser selects feasible triage actions, and a clinician override interface closes the feedback loop.

The common motivation across these architectures is robustness. Hybrid designs aim to preserve the pattern-recognition strengths of probabilistic AI while anchoring high-stakes outputs to deterministic constraints. Kalmykov and Kalmykov (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20eXplicitly%20eXplainable%20Artificial%20Intelligence.md) frame this philosophically: deterministic symbolic AI, grounded in domain axioms, should serve as the governing partner that validates and controls neural network reasoning throughout the AI lifecycle. The distinction between white-box symbolic inference and black-box probabilistic output, they argue, is not cosmetic; it is the basis of trustworthiness. In the maritime domain, Yuzui and Kaneko (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Toward%20a%20hybrid%20approach%20for%20the%20risk%20analysis%20of%20maritime%20autonomous%20surface%20ships-%20a%20systematic%20review.md) reach a similar conclusion through systematic review: combining qualitative STAMP/STPA hazard identification with quantitative Bayesian Network risk modelling produces more reliable estimates than either method in isolation. Vermaelen and Holvoet (2025) [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md), designing constraint-based planning for autonomous robots, show that embedding hard constraints directly into the planning loop improves safety and robustness without requiring exhaustive pre-specification of every possible state. In each case, the hybrid architecture is a mechanism for reliability. It is not a governance solution.

These advances address what AI *can* do. They do not address what AI is *permitted* to do as conditions change. Capability and governance address different problems. Mussi et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Human-AI%20interaction%20in%20safety-critical%20network%20infrastructures.md), surveying human-AI collaboration across power grids, railway networks, and air traffic management, identify knowledge-assisted AI, where domain rules constrain data-driven ML, as the most promising pattern for safety-critical infrastructure. But the constraints they describe are design-time choices, fixed at deployment. They do not respond to classified environmental safety state. Pitale et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/HySAFE-AI-%20Hybrid%20Safety%20Architectural%20Analysis%20Framework%20for%20AI%20Systems-%20A%20Case%20Study.md) confirm the same structural limit in autonomous driving: HySAFE-AI screens each output individually against static rules after generation. This is per-output validation. Neither system defines a permitted recommendation space *before* AI reasons, one that contracts as conditions become dangerous. The literature supplies both components of a governance architecture: a deterministic layer and a probabilistic layer. What it does not supply is the state-dependent trigger connecting them — the mechanism by which governance boundaries contract as environmental risk increases. Without it, the hybrid architecture is structurally complete but operationally static. In low-resource environments, those missing operational constraints are particularly consequential.

## 2.7 AI in Low-Resource Environments

Most AI safety architectures are designed under assumptions that do not hold in low-resource environments. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) characterises this gap in a structured review of over 100 studies: mainstream AI paradigms assume data abundance, reliable cloud connectivity, and institutional capacity for model maintenance, assumptions that are systematically violated in low- and middle-income contexts. When these assumptions fail, performance does not degrade gracefully; imported models underperform or fail entirely, not because the algorithms are wrong, but because the design premises are. Longobardi et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Peskas-%20Automated%20analytics%20for%20small-scale%2C%20data-deficient%20fisheries.md), documenting the Peskas platform for small-scale fisheries analytics deployed in Timor-Leste, illustrate the state of the art: near-real-time catch data collection, GPS vessel tracking, and automated validation are achievable in these environments. But the platform produces dashboards for human interpretation, not AI-generated decision support. Safety-state classification is absent. So is participation governance. Bhuvaneswari et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/A%20human-centered%20hybrid%20AI%20framework%20for%20optimizing%20emergency%20triage%20in%20resource-constrained%20settings.md) demonstrate that lightweight AI for safety-critical decisions is possible in resource-constrained healthcare settings, but only through deliberate choices about compute footprint, offline capability, and model architecture that standard safety-critical AI systems rarely consider.

The structural constraints of low-resource environments are not incidental; they define a different design space. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) organises them along four axes: data scarcity, compute limitations, connectivity gaps, and institutional capacity constraints. Each demands a different class of technique: TinyML for edge inference, offline-first architectures for intermittent connectivity, few-shot or physics-informed methods for sparse data. In coastal Terengganu, Malaysia, Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) give these abstractions empirical form: over half of small-scale fishers earn less than RM 1,000 per month, 60% have lower secondary education or below, and 40% operate alone at sea. Their traditional weather-prediction knowledge is eroding as climate patterns become less predictable. They are turning to weather apps, but apps provide raw data without governance logic. Current safety-critical AI frameworks largely ignore this last-mile connectivity reality. If 40% of fishers operate alone at sea with only a mobile device, any governance mechanism that classifies environmental state and restricts AI advisory scope must execute at the edge, deterministically and without cloud dependency, because connectivity is most likely to fail precisely when weather conditions deteriorate and governance is most needed. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) identifies TinyML, model–system co-design for ML inference on microcontrollers and ultra-low-power devices, as the appropriate implementation response to this constraint. The proposed architecture's governance layer satisfies this requirement directly: S = f(E) consists entirely of threshold comparisons and lookups on six parameters, executes in constant time with negligible memory, and requires no GPU, making it deployable on any smartphone processor available on a small fishing vessel. Zhao and Yuan (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20in%20Healthcare%20for%20Resource%20Limited%20Settings-%20An%20Exploration%20and%20Ethical%20Evaluation.md), evaluating AI in resource-limited healthcare, identify the same structural pattern in a different domain: data fragmentation, workforce shortages, weak regulatory oversight, and low digital literacy converge in conditions where binary governance (AI fully on or fully off) is not only technically inadequate but ethically unresolved. Whether AI governance should graduate across conditions of varying severity is raised as an open question, with no formal mechanism yet available to answer it. Recent IoT+AI deployments in small-scale aquaculture demonstrate that the technical infrastructure for environmental monitoring and AI-assisted decision-making is already feasible at this scale. Chandran et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Smart%20technologies%20in%20aquaculture-%20An%20integrated%20IoT%2C%20AI%2C%20and%20blockchain%20framework%20for%20sustainable%20growth.md) integrate IoT environmental sensors (pH, dissolved oxygen, temperature, turbidity) with AI prediction models and blockchain for data integrity in small-scale aquaculture farms. Tandel et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Smart%20Aquaculture-%20IoT%20and%20AI%20Application%20for%20Sustainable%20Fisheries.md), in a similar deployment, report a 45% reduction in fish mortality through IoT+AI-driven water quality monitoring, the strongest quantitative evidence that AI-assisted environmental monitoring is both technically viable and practically beneficial in low-resource aquatic settings. In both systems, environmental sensors feed AI prediction models that generate alerts when parameters exceed thresholds. But the alerts are binary, SMS notifications that conditions have crossed a threshold, and no governance layer sits between the environmental data and the AI's advisory output. The environment informs the AI. It does not govern it.

These constraints expose a specific cost of binary governance. When AI is fully blocked under moderate risk, users in low-resource environments have no institutional alternative to fall back on: no specialist consultation, no redundant system, no escalation pathway. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), surveying AI safety governance across industrial and transportation domains, confirm binary participation governance across all reviewed systems: no existing architecture defines intermediate operating modes or state-conditioned output restrictions. In well-resourced deployments, human expertise compensates when AI is switched off. In low-resource environments, it often cannot. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) makes this concrete: blocking a data-efficient AI system entirely under moderate risk simply transfers the decision burden to a human who may be less equipped to bear it. The cost of binary governance is asymmetric — it is highest precisely where AI resources are scarcer and alternatives are fewer. Even systems that describe themselves as "adaptive" in this space do not escape the pattern. Ogenyi et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Securing%20the%20future-%20AI-driven%20cybersecurity%20in%20the%20age%20of%20autonomous%20IoT.md), reviewing AI-driven cybersecurity for autonomous IoT, propose an "adaptive security" framework that scales protection intensity (encryption strength, monitoring frequency, anomaly detection sensitivity) based on assessed threat level. The adaptation is real, but it governs operational security parameters, not the relationship between human and AI decision-making. No threat level triggers a change in what the AI system is permitted to recommend. Adaptive security, as operationalised in the IoT literature, means tuning how aggressively the system protects itself. It does not restructure governance. This asymmetry is absent from every safety architecture reviewed. Within the class of low-resource, safety-critical environments, coastal fisheries is the domain that most directly motivates and empirically validates the proposed architecture.

## 2.8 Fisheries Decision Context

Departure decisions in small-scale coastal fisheries are made repeatedly, at short intervals, under conditions that change between trips. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) document that Terengganu fishers go to sea three to six times per week during non-monsoon periods, with each trip lasting approximately one day. Each departure begins with an environmental assessment: wind strength, sea state, rainfall, official warnings, and time of day determine whether the trip proceeds. Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) maps this decision structure among Penang fishers through 25 semi-structured interviews. Weather and safety concerns rank among the highest-rated factors for daily operational decisions, and adverse conditions halt trips altogether. The assessment is made without institutional support, without meteorological advice calibrated to small-vessel operations, and without formal risk classification. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) document the economic stakes in a study of 79 fishing households in coastal Makassar: income drops from IDR 656,000 to IDR 213,000 per trip during extreme weather, and trip frequency falls from five or six days to two or three per week. Fishers continue to sea despite recognised danger because they have no reliable alternative income. The departure decision is not made once a season. It is made three to six times a week, individually, with direct physical consequences and no structured decision guidance.

The environmental variables that govern these decisions are well-identified by the population itself. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) report that 95% of Terengganu fishers identify stronger monsoonal winds and larger waves as primary climate hazards, and 91% identify erratic rainfall and intensified weather events, matching the conditions fishers already monitor before each departure. Atacan and Düzbastılar (2023) [[notes]](../notes/Determination%20of%20risk%20perception%20in%20small-scale%20fishing%20and%20navigation.md) study risk perception among 30 small-scale fishing vessel captains using a bridge navigation simulator. Their study produces a quantitative risk hierarchy: restricted visibility records the highest mean accident probability score for sea navigation (7.90), and combined night and heavy weather produces the highest consequence score (37.03). Restricted visibility is governed primarily by daylight availability. Small vessels without electronic navigation aids depend on natural light to assess sea conditions and avoid other craft; this makes time of day a direct safety input, not merely a scheduling variable. Dominguez-Péry et al. (2023) [[notes]](../notes/A%20holistic%20view%20of%20maritime%20navigation%20accidents%20and%20risk%20indicators-%20examining%20IMO%20reports%20from%202011%20to%202021.md) confirm the pattern at scale: across 504 IMO accident investigation reports from 2011 to 2021, external environmental factors (wind, weather, and visibility) form the largest single cluster of accident risk terms (26.7% of all text segments), and vessels under 2,000 GT record the highest mean fatality rates across size categories (p = 0.01). Risk clusters at identifiable environmental thresholds. Ryu and Han (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Environment-Aware%20Multi-Sensor%20Fusion%20for%20Maritime%20Domain%20Awareness-%20A%20Comprehensive%20Review.md) provide independent physical validation from the maritime sensing literature. Their comprehensive review demonstrates that environmental conditions (wind speed, wave height, swell period, and atmospheric state) are first-order determinants of sensor reliability across all maritime sensing modalities: SAR, optical, AIS, and RF. All modalities degrade simultaneously under adverse conditions. Three distinct wind-speed regimes emerge: below 2 m/s (insufficient backscatter for SAR), 2–8 m/s (optimal detection), and above 10–12 m/s (excessive clutter overwhelms all sensors), with each regime producing qualitatively different detection behaviours. Fixed detection thresholds fail under non-stationary environmental clutter, and deep learning models suffer domain shift when trained under one regime and deployed in another. These findings confirm that environmental state is not merely a risk indicator but a direct determinant of the reliability of the information sources that any AI decision support system would depend upon. The variables constituting those thresholds (wind, rainfall, sea state, marine warning level, vessel category, and time of day) are the same parameters this population already monitors and the same inputs to the proposed environmental state vector E.

Formal safety governance for AI decision support has not been established in this domain. Haque and Al Jufaili (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Applications%20of%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Fisheries-%20From%20Data%20to%20Decisions.md), reviewing AI systems across four fisheries application domains (species monitoring, precision aquaculture, surveillance, and sensing infrastructure) confirm this across the literature: no reviewed system implements formal participation governance or advisory scope restriction conditioned on environmental safety state. Purpose-built IoT+AI systems in the closest domain confirm this pattern at the implementation level. Chandran et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Smart%20technologies%20in%20aquaculture-%20An%20integrated%20IoT%2C%20AI%2C%20and%20blockchain%20framework%20for%20sustainable%20growth.md) deploy environmental sensors feeding AI prediction models with blockchain-verified data integrity — yet the system's response architecture contains no governance layer between environmental readings and AI output. Tandel et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Smart%20Aquaculture-%20IoT%20and%20AI%20Application%20for%20Sustainable%20Fisheries.md) achieve measurable impact (45% mortality reduction) through IoT+AI water quality monitoring, but the output is a binary SMS alert when parameters cross a threshold. In both cases, environmental state serves as input to the AI system. It does not trigger changes to what the AI is permitted to recommend. The informal governance that does exist is also binary. Behavioural and institutional evidence converges on the same pattern: Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) documents weather as an implicit participation gate where adverse conditions stop trips entirely, with no documented intermediate response; Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) find the institutional equivalent: government weather warnings instruct fishers not to sail, a binary advisory with no graduated alternative. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) quantify the consequence. The dominant weather-conditioned decision pattern captured in their empirical knowledge survey is "if it's suddenly windy or the weather changes, I can't go fishing." No intermediate option is documented. This holds across all 136 respondents. Flexibility, the adaptive capacity domain covering ability to modify fishing behaviour under intermediate risk, is the weakest of the five domains measured; only 58% of fishers are willing to consider alternative strategies, and 67.6% rely solely on fishing for household income. This means the intermediate-risk decision space is genuinely unoccupied. Fishers lack an operational framework for responding to moderate conditions with a graduated answer: modified area, shorter duration, nearshore restriction. Between obvious safety and obvious danger lies a range of conditions where graduated governance would enable continued, scope-restricted participation rather than a forced binary choice. The gap between the binary governance currently available and the graduated governance this context demands is the subject of the comparative analysis that follows.

## 2.9 Comparative Analysis

The preceding sections have surveyed six bodies of literature across the governance problem: how AI decision support should behave in safety-critical environments. Each body addresses part of the problem. Evaluating them at the architectural level requires a shared analytical framework. This analysis uses four governance dimensions. The first is Level 1 governance, which determines whether the system has a mechanism that controls when AI is allowed to participate. The second is Level 2 governance, which determines whether the system has a mechanism that controls what AI is permitted to recommend. The third is unified governance, which asks whether Level 1 and Level 2 operate as a coordinated pair conditioned on the same classified state variable. Finally, environmental state conditioning asks whether the governance trigger is classified environmental safety state (a function of observable operational conditions) rather than AI performance, output content, or design-time configuration. No existing system in the reviewed corpus satisfies all four dimensions.

Safety gating systems implement Level 1 governance: they determine when AI participates. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) compute a winning region on the state space and apply a binary shield at the boundary. Bajcsy and Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) define a safety value function that overrides AI output when it crosses a safety threshold. Corsi et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Verification-Guided%20Shielding%20for%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) partition the input space via offline DNN verification, activating the shield only in verified-unsafe regions. Hamel-De le Court et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Probabilistic%20Shielding%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) extend shielding to stochastic environments with formal safety and convergence guarantees, but the probabilistic shield still intervenes or permits, with no intermediate response. Banerjee et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/CRANE-%20Reasoning%20with%20Constrained%20LLM%20Generation.md) restrict admissible output tokens via grammar intersection — a pre-hoc constraint rather than a post-hoc filter, but the governance remains binary: the model's output is either grammar-constrained or unconstrained, with Proposition 3.1 formally proving that the constrained mode reduces capability to TC⁰. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), reviewing 91 papers across six safety-critical industries, confirm this pattern holds consistently: all safety governance mechanisms reviewed are binary — AI operates or is overridden. In each case, the question asked is whether AI should act at all. The question of what AI should be permitted to recommend is not addressed. The advisory output space is the same whether conditions are safe, marginal, or dangerous. Level 1 is addressed. Level 2 is not.

Adaptive autonomy systems come closest to the proposed architecture but stop short of advisory scope restriction. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) implement a three-level traffic-light index derived from AI performance monitoring. The intermediate level triggers supervisory investigation, but AI continues to operate at the same advisory scope. The traffic-light governs how closely the human watches, not what the AI may recommend. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) is the structurally nearest comparator: a formal graduated architecture with nested permission sets E(T_k) ⊂ E(T_{k+1}) across discrete tiers, producing a containment structure formally analogous to A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE). But the tier is conditioned on verified agent robustness — an offline-assessed measure of AI trustworthiness — not on classified environmental state. Baxi's governance answers "how much can this AI be trusted?" The proposed architecture answers "what is AI permitted to recommend given current conditions?" These are orthogonal questions with different conditioning variables. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) document a three-level ODD → ROD → MRC degradation hierarchy for automated driving, where a Restricted Operational Domain narrows the vehicle's geographic scope before a minimum-risk condition stop. This is operational domain confinement, restricting where the system operates, not advisory type restriction within a fixed operational scope. As discussed in Section 2.4, Kwon et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Runtime%20Safety%20through%20Adaptive%20Shielding-%20From%20Hidden%20Parameter%20Inference%20to%20Provable%20Guarantees.md) represent the closest near-miss: environment-centric features as governance input, runtime adaptation, and a formal safety guarantee. But their SafetyScore is binary, and the adaptation is quantitative (threshold adjustment) rather than qualitative (governance mode change). Feng et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Levels%20of%20Autonomy%20for%20AI%20Agents.md), approaching from authority allocation, identify two governance dimensions, agency and autonomy, but both are configured at design time. An agent's autonomy level does not change when operating conditions deteriorate, and advisory scope restriction is absent as a third governance lever.

Guardrail systems implement a form of Level 2 governance, but statically and without environmental state conditioning. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) provide the definitive taxonomy: 13 guardrail action types across 14 pipeline targets. Chen, Kang and Li (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md) extend this to formal verification, evaluating each planned agent action against LTL rules derived from policy documents. Both systems constrain what AI produces. But the constraint is fixed: the same rules apply regardless of operational conditions. A deteriorating sea state, an approaching nightfall, a recent marine warning: none changes the guardrail configuration. The prohibited output set is conditioned on output content, not on classified environmental safety state. This is static Level 2 governance. The OWASP Agentic AI Top 10 (2025) [[notes]](../notes/OWASP%20Top%2010%20for%20Agentic%20Applications%202026.md) confirms this pattern extends to industry practice: across ten threat categories and hundreds of mitigations representing global practitioner consensus, every mitigation is binary: permit or block, allow or deny. The mechanism that would narrow the permitted recommendation space as conditions deteriorate — and widen it as conditions improve — does not exist in either the academic or practitioner literature.

A final category of systems deserves examination: IoT+AI deployments that use environmental sensors as data inputs. These systems are relevant because they demonstrate that continuous environmental monitoring is technically feasible and practically valuable, yet none uses environmental state to govern AI behaviour. Zhang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Developing%20real-time%20IoT-based%20public%20safety%20alert%20and%20emergency%20response%20systems.md), developing a real-time IoT-based public safety system, formalise the response as a binary alert function: φ(X(t)) = {1 if P_alert(X(t)) ≥ θ; 0 otherwise}. The system has continuous access to environmental sensor data, yet the governance response is a hard binary threshold with no intermediate mode. Chandran et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Smart%20technologies%20in%20aquaculture-%20An%20integrated%20IoT%2C%20AI%2C%20and%20blockchain%20framework%20for%20sustainable%20growth.md) and Tandel et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Smart%20Aquaculture-%20IoT%20and%20AI%20Application%20for%20Sustainable%20Fisheries.md), deploying IoT+AI in small-scale aquaculture, use environmental sensors (pH, dissolved oxygen, temperature, turbidity) to feed AI prediction models — but the output is a binary alert when parameters exceed thresholds. Ogenyi et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Securing%20the%20future-%20AI-driven%20cybersecurity%20in%20the%20age%20of%20autonomous%20IoT.md) propose an "adaptive security" framework for IoT that scales operational parameters (encryption, monitoring) based on threat level, but the adaptation governs security intensity, not the relationship between human and AI in decision-making. Across all four systems, environmental state serves as *input* to the AI system. It never serves as a *governance trigger* that changes what the AI is permitted to recommend. This environment-as-input-not-governance pattern is consistent across domains, from public safety to aquaculture to IoT cybersecurity.

Safety gating, guardrails, adaptive autonomy, and IoT+AI systems each address only one part of the governance problem; none combines participation control with advisory-scope restriction under the same environmental state. Safety gating resolves participation but is silent on advisory scope. Guardrails restrict outputs but apply the same rules regardless of environmental conditions. Adaptive autonomy varies monitoring intensity or operational domain, yet the range of what the AI may recommend stays constant. No architecture identified in this review implements G(S) and A_AI(S) as a unified pair conditioned on the same S = f(E). Four independent systematic reviews converge on this conclusion from different angles. At the empirical level, Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) and Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) find binary governance consistent across hundreds of papers spanning collaborative intelligence, automotive, avionics, railway, and industrial domains — no architecture in either corpus restricts AI recommendation types based on classified safety state. At the formal methods level, Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) find that every method reviewed across 46 studies implements binary safety boundaries. At the institutional level, Attard-Frost and Lyons (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20governance%20systems-%20A%20multi-scale%20analysis%20framework%2C%20empirical%20findings%2C%20and%20future%20directions.%20AI%20and%20Ethics.md) map 610 governance topics in a national AI governance system and confirm that runtime environmental state classification, state-conditioned advisory scope restriction, and graduated CAUTION modes fall entirely outside the governance topics mapped. The gap is not an artefact of one literature stream. It is structural. It appears at the technical, formal methods, and institutional governance layers simultaneously. The comparative analysis confirms a structural gap — consistent across every category of system reviewed. What follows consolidates the cumulative evidence and states the research question this gap generates.

## 2.10 Literature Summary

The eight bodies of literature reviewed (AI decision support, safety gating, human-AI authority, adaptive autonomy, guardrails, hybrid AI, low-resource AI, and fisheries decision context) have collectively mapped the governance problem from different angles. The technical bodies have matured specific governance dimensions: safety gating delivers formally verified participation control; guardrails deliver rich output-restriction toolkits with 13 documented action types; adaptive autonomy demonstrates that governance posture need not be static; human-AI authority frameworks establish that oversight allocation is a distinct question from output restriction. Safety gating, guardrails, and adaptive autonomy have each advanced one governance dimension; no existing system addresses all three dimensions under the same environmental state. Safety gating controls when AI acts without restricting what it recommends. Guardrails restrict what AI recommends without conditioning on operational state. Adaptive autonomy varies monitoring intensity without restricting advisory scope. The enabling bodies (hybrid AI, low-resource AI, and the fisheries domain) establish that the gap has direct deployment consequences. In resource-constrained, safety-critical environments, binary governance transfers decision burden to humans who are less equipped to bear it. Graduated guidance would matter most under intermediate-risk conditions. These are precisely the conditions where no governance-aware AI decision support exists. External evidence from IoT+AI deployments in aquaculture and public safety reinforces this conclusion: even systems with continuous environmental sensor data and real-time AI prediction treat environment as input to the AI, never as a trigger for governance change.

The collective limitation is binary governance: AI either acts or is blocked, with no intermediate mode in which it participates within a formally restricted advisory scope. This is confirmed at every level of analysis examined in this review. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) find binary governance consistent across all 91 safety-critical collaborative intelligence papers spanning six industries. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) reach the same conclusion across 11 international frontier AI safety frameworks developed by the world's leading AI organisations. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) confirm the pattern across 46 formal methods studies. The architecture required to address this gap, a governance pair (G(S), A_AI(S)) conditioned on classified environmental safety state S = f(E) with formal safety properties proved across that conditioning, does not appear in any reviewed body of literature. This synthesis leads directly to the research gap examined next: no architecture in the reviewed literature defines a two-level governance pair conditioned on classified environmental safety state — and no system implements the intermediate mode this gap demands.

## 2.11 Research Gap

This review finds no architecture that jointly conditions two governance functions on the same classified environmental safety state S: a participation gate G(S) and an admissible recommendation space A_AI(S). Across the reviewed corpus, no system classifies observable environmental conditions into a tripartite state S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE} using a deterministic function S = f(E) over a formally specified parameter vector. The containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅, defined using set containment over a finite recommendation type space R, ensuring that what AI may recommend narrows as environmental danger increases and reduces to the empty set in the most dangerous state — was not observed in any reviewed system. The reviewed literature does not contain a formal proof of the Safety Dominance Property: AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S) for all environmental states E. Most critically, no system identified in this review implements the CAUTION mode — the intermediate operational state in which AI participates in decision support, but only within a formally restricted advisory scope, because conditions are elevated but not yet prohibitive. Every mechanism examined treats AI participation as binary. The system operates at full advisory scope or not at all. Zhang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Developing%20real-time%20IoT-based%20public%20safety%20alert%20and%20emergency%20response%20systems.md) provide the most explicit formalisation of this ceiling: their Equation 4 defines safety response as a hard binary function over continuous environmental sensor data, with no intermediate output. The OWASP Agentic AI Top 10 (2025) [[notes]](../notes/OWASP%20Top%2010%20for%20Agentic%20Applications%202026.md) confirms this is not only an academic pattern but an industry standard: hundreds of security mitigations across ten agentic AI threat categories, reflecting global practitioner consensus, are uniformly binary.

When AI is blocked under elevated risk, decision support is removed at precisely the moment it is most needed. In low-resource environments, this transfers the entire cognitive burden to operators who lack redundant institutional safeguards: no specialist to call, no escalation pathway, no alternative system. The other failure mode is equally costly: allowing AI to operate at unchanged advisory scope as conditions deteriorate means the same precise departure times, duration estimates, and route recommendations are presented whether conditions are calm or dangerous. This is false precision under conditions that cannot support it. The CAUTION mode resolves this dilemma: AI continues providing decision support when conditions are elevated but not prohibitive, within a restricted advisory scope covering recommendation types that remain appropriate under those conditions — nearshore areas rather than open-water routes, shorter durations, weather-conditional itineraries. This is not a marginal refinement of existing approaches. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) implement three operational levels, but their intermediate level governs supervisory monitoring intensity, not advisory scope. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) implements nested permission sets with the right containment structure, but conditions on agent robustness, a static and offline-assessed property, not on classified environmental state. Kwon et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Runtime%20Safety%20through%20Adaptive%20Shielding-%20From%20Hidden%20Parameter%20Inference%20to%20Provable%20Guarantees.md) come closest: their adaptive shielding uses environment-centric features as governance input, adapts at runtime, and carries a formal safety proof — but the SafetyScore remains binary, and the adaptation adjusts threshold sensitivity rather than switching governance mode. None of these systems implements a mode in which an AI decision support system participates under reduced advisory scope because the environment, not the AI, is in an elevated risk condition.

Three research gaps follow from this analysis. The first is architectural: no formally defined mechanism unifies participation governance and advisory scope governance under the same classified environmental state, with proved safety properties that hold across all conditions. Empirically, no study compares a graduated two-level governance architecture against binary governance in a safety-critical deployment context, establishing whether graduated governance produces better decision outcomes without compromising safety. The third is socio-technical: no study examines user response to the CAUTION mode as a distinct operational state — whether users calibrate their trust and reliance appropriately when AI participates under advisory scope restriction, rather than either operating at full scope or not operating at all. Therefore, this research proposes a graduated safety-state-gated architecture for AI decision support that defines how safety conditions govern both AI participation and advisory scope.
