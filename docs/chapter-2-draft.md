# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 AI Decision Support in Safety-Critical Systems

Artificial intelligence is increasingly used to support human decision-making in safety-critical industries. Applications span automotive, avionics, railway, industrial robotics, and maritime domains, where AI systems provide predictions, risk assessments, and operational recommendations (Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md)). Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), in a systematic review of 91 studies on collaborative intelligence, identify two primary interaction modes: machine-assists-human and human-assists-machine. In both cases, AI functions in an advisory capacity. It augments human judgement rather than replacing it, because the complexity and consequence of safety-critical decisions still require human oversight. The International AI Safety Report notes that general-purpose AI capabilities are expanding rapidly, with systems now performing reasoning, planning, and autonomous action at scales not anticipated even two years prior (Bengio et al., 2026 [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md)). As a result, the scope and reach of AI advisory systems in safety-critical domains is growing — and with it, the consequences of getting AI behaviour wrong.

The benefits are well documented. AI reduces cognitive load by processing multiple data streams simultaneously. It identifies patterns across larger datasets than humans can evaluate (Ramos et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md)). In practice, it also improves situational awareness in time-pressured environments — this happens because it can integrate heterogeneous inputs, such as sensor readings, forecasts, and historical records, faster than a human operator. Bloomfield and Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) make a stronger claim: AI enables functional capabilities, such as real-time perception and dynamic risk estimation, that conventional software simply cannot replicate. Bengio, Hinton et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Managing%20extreme%20AI%20risks%20amid%20rapid%20progress.md), in a consensus statement by 25 leading AI researchers, acknowledge that AI has produced substantial benefits across scientific research, healthcare, and engineering. None of this is disputed.

The problem is that AI systems also produce incorrect outputs — and in safety-critical environments, incorrect recommendations do not simply degrade performance. They anchor human decisions toward unsafe outcomes. Wen et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Risk%20Perception%20in%20Complex%20Systems-%20A%20Comparative%20Analysis%20of%20Process%20Control%20and%20Autonomous%20Vehicle%20Failures.md) demonstrate this empirically. Analysing 60 real-world accident reports across process control and autonomous vehicle domains, they find that human intervention was ineffective in 83.3% of process control cases and 66.7% of autonomous vehicle cases. What is striking is that the dominant failure mode was not technical malfunction. It was human–automation interaction breakdown: over-reliance, mode confusion, passive monitoring. This shows that the risk lies not only in the AI output itself but in how humans respond to it. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) identify a related concern from the formal methods side. Reviewing 46 studies, they observe that nearly all safety enforcement is applied reactively — after AI produces output — rather than proactively restricting what AI is permitted to produce. Bloomfield and Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) put it plainly: AI and ML components are opaque, experimentally derived, and cannot satisfy classical fault-avoidance assurance requirements. In other words, when AI operates in safety-critical contexts, the system must account not only for what AI gets right but for what happens when it gets things wrong. This raises a deeper question — not only how to verify AI outputs, but whether AI should be allowed to produce them under all conditions.

Current research addresses this challenge primarily through two strategies: improving AI accuracy and improving AI explainability. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), surveying safety techniques across industrial and transportation domains, identify safety bags, safety monitors, and safety envelopes as the dominant runtime mechanisms. All three verify or filter AI outputs after generation. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) confirm this pattern across a broader scope: they survey eight categories of formal methods — reachability, SMT verification, model checking, runtime verification, shielding, control barrier functions, risk verification, and MILP/ILP — and find that none governs AI participation or advisory scope as a function of classified operational conditions. This means that the field has focused on making AI outputs safer or more transparent, but has not asked a more fundamental question: under what conditions should AI participate in decision-making at all? And when it does participate, what should it be permitted to recommend? The governance of AI behaviour under varying safety conditions — not just the correctness of AI outputs — is the central concern of this review.

## 2.2 Deterministic Safety Constraints and Safety Gating

The literature's first response to the problem of unsafe AI behaviour has been deterministic safety constraints. These mechanisms sit alongside or around the AI system and enforce safety properties that the AI itself cannot guarantee. Three architectural patterns dominate. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) formalise *shields* — runtime mechanisms synthesised from LTL safety specifications and environment models that intercept unsafe actions before they reach the environment. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) propose Guaranteed Safe AI, a framework built on three components: a world model, a safety specification, and a verifier that produces auditable proof certificates before AI output is deployed. Bajcsy and Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) take a control-theoretic approach, defining a Human-AI Safety Filter that monitors AI outputs and intervenes at the last possible moment to prevent trajectory-level safety violations. What these approaches share is a common structural principle: a deterministic layer constrains a probabilistic AI layer, because the AI cannot be trusted to self-regulate.

The mechanism across all three patterns is a binary gate. The shield either permits the agent's action or replaces it with a safe alternative (Könighofer et al., 2025 [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md)). The verifier either certifies the output or blocks deployment (Dalrymple et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md)). The safety filter either lets the action pass or overrides it with a fallback policy (Bajcsy & Fisac, 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md)). More recent work refines this pattern but does not change its binary character. Corsi et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Verification-Guided%20Shielding%20for%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) use formal DNN verification to partition the input space into provably safe and potentially unsafe regions, activating the shield only where needed. This reduces overhead by 25–71%. However, the shield itself still operates as a binary allow-or-replace gate. Abella et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SAFEXPLAIN-%20a%20Complete%20Approach%20Towards%20Trustworthy%20AI-Based%20Safety-Critical%20Systems.md), in the EU SAFEXPLAIN project, implement a supervision function that bounds AI operations within a predefined safety envelope and can switch to a non-AI fallback subsystem. Vermaelen and Holvoet (2025) [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md) use constraint-based planning with state rules that exclude any action whose foreseeable outcomes violate safety conditions. In every case, the governance decision is binary: safe or unsafe, permit or block, continue or fall back. There is no intermediate mode.

This binary pattern is not an artefact of a few selected papers. It holds across the literature at scale. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), reviewing 91 collaborative intelligence studies across safety-critical industries, find no system that varies its advisory scope based on classified safety state. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) survey 46 formal methods studies and identify shielding, runtime verification, and control barrier functions as the dominant enforcement mechanisms — all binary. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), mapping 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks, confirm that risk tiering triggers binary deployment decisions: capability thresholds either permit or restrict AI deployment, with no graduated advisory scope. Odriozola-Olalde et al. (2023) [[notes]](../notes/Shielded%20Reinforcement%20Learning-%20A%20review%20of%20reactive%20methods%20for%20safe%20learning.md) conduct a dedicated review of all shielded reinforcement learning methods and classify them into three Safety Levels — but all three levels implement binary per-action gates. No reviewed method restricts the *types* of actions an agent may propose as a function of environmental state. The absence of an intermediate governance mode is consistent and cross-domain.

These safety constraints address an important question — whether AI should act — but they leave a second question unanswered: what should AI be allowed to recommend when it does act? Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) classify AI safety compliance into three classes, where Class II systems use a certified safety bag to monitor an uncertified AI item. Even in this most structured pattern, the safety bag checks whether AI output is safe. It does not restrict the scope of what the AI is permitted to produce in the first place. In the terminology of this review, existing safety constraints implement Level 1 governance — controlling whether AI participates in decision-making — but provide no Level 2 governance over advisory scope. If the gate is open, the AI operates at full capability regardless of risk conditions. A separate question therefore arises: how should decision authority be allocated between human and AI, and does that allocation solve the advisory scope problem?

## 2.3 Human–AI Decision Authority

A parallel body of literature addresses the allocation of decision authority between human and AI. The central idea is that safety depends not only on constraining AI actions but on defining the respective roles of human and machine in the decision process. Several classification frameworks have been proposed. Porter et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/INSYTE-%20A%20Classification%20Framework%20for%20Traditional%20to%20Agentic%20AI%20Systems.md) develop INSYTE, an eight-dimension framework that classifies AI systems across a spectrum from traditional rule-based tools to fully agentic systems. Two of its dimensions — independence from intervention and independence from oversight — directly characterise how much human governance exists over AI behaviour. Bach, Kristiansen et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Unpacking%20Human-AI%20Interaction%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Systematic%20Literature%20Review.md), reviewing 24 empirical studies of human-AI interaction in safety-critical industries, identify four primary roles for AI: collaborative AI, AI-assisted decision-making, human-controlled AI, and emotional AI. AI-assisted decision-making — where the AI advises and the human retains final authority — dominates, appearing in 16 of 24 studies. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) organise their 91-paper review around the same distinction: machine assists human, or human assists machine. Across all three frameworks, the organising question is *who decides* — the human, the AI, or some combination.

The mechanism for managing this authority varies. In some systems, authority is allocated at design time and remains fixed during operation. In others, it shifts dynamically based on task context. Saup et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/From%20pilots%20to%20decision%20systems-%20embedding%20generative%20AI%20into%20strategic%20decision-making%20through%20a%20socio-technical%20and%20governance%20lens.md) propose four admission gates — quality, provenance, explainability, and accountability — that determine whether AI outputs are admitted into human strategic deliberation. These gates are organisational checkpoints: they filter AI outputs *after* generation, based on whether the output meets governance criteria. Madsen and Kim (2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md), reviewing AI decision transparency for autonomous shipping, find that the dominant approach is transparency-based: colour-coded risk displays, route visualisations, and layered information interfaces that help operators understand AI reasoning. The assumption is that if the human can understand and override the AI, safety is maintained. Leppinen et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/A%20Stage-Gate%20Decision%20Process%20for%20Guiding%20the%20Development%20of%20AI%20Solutions%20for%20Preventive%20Maintenance.md) apply a stage-gate process with go/no-go decisions, but at the development level — governing which AI solutions proceed through the pipeline, not what a deployed AI system is permitted to output at runtime.

These frameworks address an important dimension of AI safety, but they share a structural limitation. They govern *who decides* — how much authority the human retains, how much the AI exercises — but they do not constrain the range of outputs the AI can generate. Porter et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/INSYTE-%20A%20Classification%20Framework%20for%20Traditional%20to%20Agentic%20AI%20Systems.md) classify intervention and oversight independence on a scale, but this scale describes how much a human is involved, not what types of recommendations the AI is allowed to generate. A system rated at INSYTE Level 3 (AI makes decisions with human oversight) still produces the same outputs regardless of operational risk. Bach, Kristiansen et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Unpacking%20Human-AI%20Interaction%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Systematic%20Literature%20Review.md) find no system in their review that restricts AI output scope based on classified safety state — one study notes that users relied on AI more in low-risk than high-risk conditions, but this is informal behavioural adaptation, not architectural governance. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) confirm this across a larger corpus: authority allocation frameworks do not vary AI advisory scope. The AI produces the same recommendations whether conditions are safe or dangerous.

This gap matters because authority allocation and advisory scope governance solve different problems. Knowing that the human has final decision authority does not help if the AI presents a full set of recommendations — including timing optimisations and trip duration estimates — under conditions where such precision is unreliable. Madsen and Kim (2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md) illustrate this clearly: autonomous shipping systems use colour-coded risk levels to vary the *display* presented to operators, but do not vary the *types of recommendations* the AI generates. The governance question in that literature is "can the human understand and override the AI?" — not "what should the AI be permitted to recommend given current conditions?" Authority allocation governs the human side of the interaction. What remains missing is governance of the AI side — a mechanism that restricts the advisory scope of the AI itself, based on classified operational conditions. If static authority allocation cannot provide this, the natural next step is systems that adapt AI behaviour based on risk at runtime.

## 2.4 Adaptive Autonomy and Risk-Based Systems

A growing body of work moves beyond static authority allocation by designing systems that adapt their behaviour in response to changing risk conditions. The idea is straightforward: if conditions deteriorate, the system should respond — by increasing monitoring, reducing autonomy, or switching to a safer operating mode. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) propose a traffic-light degradation index for AI in safety-critical systems, classifying AI performance into three levels: green (nominal), orange (degradation detected), and red (threshold exceeded). At red, the system switches to a conventional non-AI backup controller. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md), reviewing safety evidence methods for automated driving, identify a similar hierarchy: Operational Design Domain (ODD), Restricted Operational Domain (ROD), and Minimal Risk Condition (MRC). The system operates nominally within its ODD, degrades to a restricted domain when capabilities are reduced, and transitions to a minimal risk state as a last resort. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) formalises a K-tier graduated permission architecture for AI economic agents, where a gate function maps verified robustness scores to discrete permission tiers — each tier defining a different set of permitted actions. All three approaches share the principle that governance should be graduated rather than binary.

The mechanisms differ in detail but follow a common pattern: classify a condition, then adjust system behaviour accordingly. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) compute the traffic-light index from performance metrics, outlier detection, and concept drift monitoring — all non-AI statistical methods applied to the AI's own behaviour. At Level 2 (orange), the supervisory component conducts thorough checks and investigates root causes. At Level 3 (red), it switches to the backup. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) uses a weakest-link principle: the agent's tier is determined by its worst robustness dimension, with a formal proof that economic exposure is bounded by the verified tier. Li et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Safety-Enhanced%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning%20for%20Autonomous%20Driving-%20Dare%20to%20Make%20Mistakes%20to%20Learn%20Better%20and%20Faster.md), working in autonomous driving, apply a dynamic risk threshold that evaluates anticipated risk per action and replaces unsafe actions with safe alternatives — reducing collision rates by over 92%. These systems demonstrate that graduated and risk-responsive governance is technically feasible.

The limitation, however, is consistent across all three. What changes between levels is *monitoring intensity*, *control authority*, or *individual action correction* — not the scope of what the AI is permitted to recommend. In Flehmig et al.'s (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) traffic-light framework, the AI produces the same types of outputs at Level 1 (green) and Level 2 (orange). The difference is purely supervisory: routine checks versus thorough investigation. The AI's recommendation space does not shrink. Baxi's (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) architecture comes closest to advisory scope restriction — permission sets genuinely vary by tier — but the classification is based on the agent's own verified robustness, not on environmental conditions. A highly robust agent receives broad permissions regardless of whether it operates in a safe or dangerous environment. Li et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Safety-Enhanced%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning%20for%20Autonomous%20Driving-%20Dare%20to%20Make%20Mistakes%20to%20Learn%20Better%20and%20Faster.md) correct individual unsafe actions but never restrict the *types* of actions the agent may propose. The agent always generates full-scope outputs; only specific violations are intercepted.

This reveals the architectural gap more precisely. Existing graduated systems adapt *how the system is supervised* or *how individual actions are checked*, but none formally restricts *what the AI is allowed to recommend* as a function of classified environmental state. Flehmig et al.'s (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) traffic-light model is the closest structural analogue — three classified levels, non-AI classification, different responses per level — but the intermediate level governs the *supervisor*, not the *AI*. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) provide the formal machinery for constraining AI at runtime through shields, but those shields operate per-action, not per-state. No architecture in this literature unifies participation governance (whether AI acts) with advisory scope governance (what AI is permitted to recommend) under the same classified environmental state. Before turning to the application domain, two further bodies of literature require review: AI guardrails and hybrid AI systems.

## 2.5 AI Governance and Guardrails

A separate strand of research addresses AI governance through guardrails — runtime mechanisms that intercept, evaluate, and filter AI outputs to prevent unsafe or undesirable behaviour. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md), drawing on the Swiss Cheese Model from accident theory, propose a multi-layered guardrail architecture for foundation model agents, where each layer addresses a different quality attribute across different pipeline stages — prompts, intermediate results, and final outputs. Their taxonomy identifies 13 distinct guardrail actions, including block, filter, flag, modify, validate, and human intervention. Chen et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20Safety%20Landscape%20for%20Large%20Language%20Models-%20Taxonomy%2C%20State-of-the-art%2C%20and%20Future%20Directions.md) formalise the guardrail system as an AI pipeline — an input filtering module, the LLM, and an output filtering module — and define two AI Safety Principles: output constraint (the output space must be disjoint from prohibited sets) and runtime constraint (the system must operate within predefined requirements). Wang et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/AgentSpec-%20Customizable%20Runtime%20Enforcement%20for%20Safe%20and%20Reliable%20LLM%20Agents.md) develop AgentSpec, a domain-specific language for runtime enforcement that allows developers to define structured rules composed of triggers, predicates, and enforcement actions — preventing over 90% of unsafe code executions in their evaluation. The guardrail literature is technically mature and offers a rich vocabulary for describing output governance.

The core mechanism is post-hoc filtering. The AI generates an output, and the guardrail evaluates it against predefined rules before the output reaches the user or the environment. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) make this explicit: guardrails are "mechanisms integrated into an agent's architecture to safeguard its behavior during runtime." Chen, Kang and Li (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md), in SHIELDAGENT, push this further by extracting LTL rules from policy documents and using formal verification to evaluate each agent action against the relevant rule circuits. The result is a binary safety label per action — safe or unsafe — with violated rules and explanations. Liang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md), applying the Guaranteed Safe AI framework to semantic communication networks, implement a gatekeeper that filters AI outputs falling below a quality threshold, achieving a 54% improvement in output accuracy. In each case, the guardrail sits *between* the AI and the downstream consumer. It does not change what the AI attempts to produce — it intercepts what the AI has already produced.

This post-hoc architecture has a structural consequence. Because guardrails evaluate outputs after generation, they apply the same filtering rules regardless of environmental conditions. Shamsujjoha et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) acknowledge "context-dependent rules" as one of four rule types, but context here refers to data or deployment context — user location, regulatory jurisdiction — not classified environmental safety state. The guardrail configuration does not change when conditions become dangerous. Chen et al.'s (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20Safety%20Landscape%20for%20Large%20Language%20Models-%20Taxonomy%2C%20State-of-the-art%2C%20and%20Future%20Directions.md) AI Safety Principle I formalises this as a static constraint: the output space must be disjoint from prohibited sets. The prohibited set does not vary with operational state. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), assessing the state of risk management across 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks, conclude that "no combination of methods ensures the high reliability required in critical domains." Part of the reason is that these methods treat safety as a fixed property of the AI system rather than a dynamic function of the operating environment.

The distinction between post-hoc filtering and pre-hoc output space definition is critical for this review. Guardrails ask: "Is this specific output acceptable?" The question this review poses is different: "What *types* of outputs should the AI be permitted to generate, given current conditions?" Wang et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/AgentSpec-%20Customizable%20Runtime%20Enforcement%20for%20Safe%20and%20Reliable%20LLM%20Agents.md) illustrate the difference clearly. AgentSpec evaluates each planned action against applicable rules — but the agent's action space remains the same regardless of environmental state. Unsafe actions are caught individually. There is no mechanism that narrows the admissible output space *before* the AI reasons, as a function of classified safety conditions. Abella et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SAFEXPLAIN-%20a%20Complete%20Approach%20Towards%20Trustworthy%20AI-Based%20Safety-Critical%20Systems.md) come closer with a supervision function that constrains AI operations within a predefined safety envelope — but whether the envelope varies by classified state is not specified or formalised. What is needed is not more sophisticated filtering after AI generates output, but a governance mechanism that defines the permitted output space *before* AI operates — and that varies this space based on classified environmental safety state. Governance defines what AI should be permitted to do. The mechanism by which AI combines deterministic rules with probabilistic inference determines what is technically possible to govern.

## 2.6 Hybrid AI Systems

Hybrid AI systems combine rule-based and probabilistic components to address the limitations of purely statistical models in safety-critical domains. The most direct architectural response is strict role separation. Castagnone & Nitti (2026) [[notes]](../notes/A%20Neuro-Symbolic%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Deterministic%20Reliability%20in%20AI-Assisted%20Structural%20Engineering-%20The%20SYNAPSE%20Architecture.md) demonstrate this in the SYNAPSE architecture: a neural component handles natural language understanding and result presentation, while a deterministic symbolic engine performs all safety-critical calculations. The design principle is unambiguous — the neural component is never permitted to compute safety-critical outputs directly. Punzi et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/AI%2C%20Meet%20Human-%20Learning%20Paradigms%20for%20Hybrid%20Decision-Making%20Systems.md) survey this integration more broadly, identifying three paradigms of increasing human-machine coupling: human oversight (accepting or rejecting machine predictions), learning to abstain (the machine learns when to defer), and bidirectional collaboration where human and machine co-learn. In emergency medicine, Bhuvaneswari et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/A%20human-centered%20hybrid%20AI%20framework%20for%20optimizing%20emergency%20triage%20in%20resource-constrained%20settings.md) deploy a similar hybrid logic in resource-constrained settings: a rule-based cognitive module encodes clinical heuristics, a lightweight CNN handles risk prediction, a resource-aware optimiser selects feasible triage actions, and a clinician override interface closes the feedback loop.

The common motivation across these architectures is robustness. Hybrid designs aim to preserve the pattern-recognition strengths of probabilistic AI while anchoring high-stakes outputs to deterministic constraints. Kalmykov & Kalmykov (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20eXplicitly%20eXplainable%20Artificial%20Intelligence.md) frame this philosophically: deterministic symbolic AI, grounded in domain axioms, should serve as the governing partner that validates and controls neural network reasoning throughout the AI lifecycle. The distinction between white-box symbolic inference and black-box probabilistic output, they argue, is not cosmetic — it is the basis of trustworthiness. In the maritime domain, Yuzui & Kaneko (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Toward%20a%20hybrid%20approach%20for%20the%20risk%20analysis%20of%20maritime%20autonomous%20surface%20ships-%20a%20systematic%20review.md) reach a similar conclusion through systematic review: combining qualitative STAMP/STPA hazard identification with quantitative Bayesian Network risk modelling produces more reliable estimates than either method in isolation. Vermaelen & Holvoet (2025) [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md), designing constraint-based planning for autonomous robots, show that embedding hard constraints directly into the planning loop improves safety and robustness without requiring exhaustive pre-specification of every possible state. In each case, the hybrid architecture is a mechanism for reliability — not a solution to the governance problem.

These advances address the quality of AI outputs. They do not address the scope of what AI may produce, or the conditions under which it should participate at all. Mussi et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Human-AI%20interaction%20in%20safety-critical%20network%20infrastructures.md), surveying human-AI collaboration across power grids, railway networks, and air traffic management, identify knowledge-assisted AI — where domain rules constrain data-driven ML — as the most promising pattern for safety-critical infrastructure. But the constraints they describe are design-time architectural choices. They are fixed at deployment; they do not respond to classified environmental safety state. Pitale et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/HySAFE-AI-%20Hybrid%20Safety%20Architectural%20Analysis%20Framework%20for%20AI%20Systems-%20A%20Case%20Study.md) illustrate the same structural limit in autonomous driving: HySAFE-AI combines a foundation model with a Policy Monitor for out-of-distribution detection and a Safety Evaluator that applies rule-based and physics-derived checks. Unsafe trajectories are rejected after generation. This is per-output validation — each output is screened individually against static rules. What neither system does is define a permitted recommendation space *before* AI reasons, one that contracts as conditions become dangerous. Hybrid architecture determines what AI *can* do. It does not determine what AI is *permitted* to do as conditions change. Hybrid AI is the enabling architecture. The deployment context determines the constraints. In low-resource environments, those constraints are particularly severe.

## 2.7 AI in Low-Resource Environments

Most AI safety architectures are designed under assumptions that do not hold in low-resource environments. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) characterises this gap in a structured review of over 100 studies: mainstream AI paradigms assume data abundance, reliable cloud connectivity, and institutional capacity for model maintenance — assumptions that are systematically violated in low- and middle-income contexts. When these assumptions fail, performance does not degrade gracefully; imported models underperform or fail entirely, not because the algorithms are wrong, but because the design premises are. Longobardi et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Peskas-%20Automated%20analytics%20for%20small-scale%2C%20data-deficient%20fisheries.md), documenting the Peskas platform for small-scale fisheries analytics deployed in Timor-Leste, illustrate the state of the art: near-real-time catch data collection, GPS vessel tracking, and automated validation are achievable in these environments. But the platform produces dashboards for human interpretation, not AI-generated decision support. No safety-state classification exists; no AI participation governance has been built. Bhuvaneswari et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/A%20human-centered%20hybrid%20AI%20framework%20for%20optimizing%20emergency%20triage%20in%20resource-constrained%20settings.md) demonstrate that lightweight AI for safety-critical decisions is possible in resource-constrained healthcare settings, but only through deliberate choices about compute footprint, offline capability, and model architecture that standard safety-critical AI systems rarely consider.

The structural constraints of low-resource environments are not incidental — they define a different design space. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) organises them along four axes: data scarcity, compute limitations, connectivity gaps, and institutional capacity constraints. Each demands a different class of technique — TinyML for edge inference, offline-first architectures for intermittent connectivity, few-shot or physics-informed methods for sparse data. In coastal Terengganu, Malaysia, Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) give these abstractions empirical form: over half of small-scale fishers earn less than RM 1,000 per month, 60% have lower secondary education or below, and 40% operate alone at sea. Their traditional weather-prediction knowledge is eroding as climate patterns become less predictable. They are turning to weather apps — but apps provide raw data without governance logic. Zhao & Yuan (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20in%20Healthcare%20for%20Resource%20Limited%20Settings-%20An%20Exploration%20and%20Ethical%20Evaluation.md), evaluating AI in resource-limited healthcare, identify the same structural pattern in a different domain: data fragmentation, workforce shortages, weak regulatory oversight, and low digital literacy converge in conditions where binary governance — AI fully on or fully off — is not only technically inadequate but ethically unresolved. Whether AI governance should graduate across conditions of varying severity is raised as an open question, with no formal mechanism yet available to answer it.

These constraints expose a specific cost of binary governance. When AI is fully blocked under moderate risk, users in low-resource environments have no institutional alternative to fall back on — no specialist consultation, no redundant system, no escalation pathway. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), surveying AI safety governance across industrial and transportation domains, confirm binary participation governance as universal: no existing architecture defines intermediate operating modes or state-conditioned output restrictions. In well-resourced deployments, human expertise compensates when AI is switched off. In low-resource environments, it often cannot. Katende (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Rethinking%20data-efficient%20artificial%20intelligence%20for%20low-resource%20settings.md) makes this concrete: blocking a data-efficient AI system entirely under moderate risk simply transfers the decision burden to a human who may be less equipped to bear it. The cost of binary governance is asymmetric — it is highest precisely where AI resources are scarcer and alternatives are fewer. No current AI safety architecture is designed with this asymmetry in mind. Within the class of low-resource, safety-critical environments, coastal fisheries is the domain that most directly motivates and empirically validates the proposed architecture.

## 2.8 Fisheries Decision Context

Departure decisions in small-scale coastal fisheries are made repeatedly, at short intervals, under conditions that change between trips. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) document that Terengganu fishers go to sea three to six times per week during non-monsoon periods, with each trip lasting approximately one day. Each departure begins with an environmental assessment: wind strength, sea state, rainfall, official warnings, and time of day determine whether the trip proceeds. Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) maps this decision structure among Penang fishers through 25 semi-structured interviews. Weather and safety concerns rank among the highest-rated factors for daily operational decisions, and adverse conditions halt trips altogether. The assessment is made without institutional support, without meteorological advice calibrated to small-vessel operations, and without formal risk classification. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) document the economic stakes in a study of 79 fishing households in coastal Makassar: income drops from IDR 656,000 to IDR 213,000 per trip during extreme weather, and trip frequency falls from five or six days to two or three per week. Fishers continue to sea despite recognised danger because they have no reliable alternative income. The departure decision is not made once a season. It is made three to six times a week, individually, with direct physical consequences and no structured decision guidance.

The environmental variables that govern these decisions are well-identified by the population itself. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) report that 95% of Terengganu fishers identify stronger monsoonal winds and larger waves as primary climate hazards, and 91% identify erratic rainfall and intensified weather events — matching the conditions fishers already monitor before each departure. Atacan & Düzbastılar (2023) [[notes]](../notes/Determination%20of%20risk%20perception%20in%20small-scale%20fishing%20and%20navigation.md) study risk perception among 30 small-scale fishing vessel captains using a bridge navigation simulator. Their study produces a quantitative risk hierarchy: restricted visibility records the highest mean accident probability score for sea navigation (7.90), and combined night and heavy weather produces the highest consequence score (37.03). Restricted visibility is governed primarily by daylight availability. Small vessels without electronic navigation aids depend on natural light to assess sea conditions and avoid other craft; this makes time of day a direct safety input, not merely a scheduling variable. Dominguez-Péry et al. (2023) [[notes]](../notes/A%20holistic%20view%20of%20maritime%20navigation%20accidents%20and%20risk%20indicators-%20examining%20IMO%20reports%20from%202011%20to%202021.md) confirm the pattern at scale: across 504 IMO accident investigation reports from 2011 to 2021, external environmental factors — wind, weather, and visibility — form the largest single cluster of accident risk terms (26.7% of all text segments), and vessels under 2,000 GT record the highest mean fatality rates across size categories (p = 0.01). Risk clusters at identifiable environmental thresholds. The variables constituting those thresholds — wind, rainfall, sea state, marine warning level, vessel condition, and time of day — are the same parameters this population already monitors and the same inputs to the proposed environmental state vector E.

No formal safety governance mechanism exists for AI decision support in this domain. The informal governance that does exist is binary. Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) documents weather as an implicit participation gate: adverse conditions stop trips, with no documented intermediate response in which fishers modify operational scope rather than cancelling. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) document the institutional equivalent: government weather warnings instruct fishers not to sail — a binary advisory with no graduated alternative. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) provide quantitative evidence of the consequence. The dominant weather-conditioned decision pattern captured in their empirical knowledge survey is "if it's suddenly windy or the weather changes, I can't go fishing." No intermediate option is documented across 136 respondents. Flexibility — the adaptive capacity domain covering ability to modify fishing behaviour under intermediate risk — is the weakest of the five domains measured; only 58% of fishers are willing to consider alternative strategies, and 67.6% rely solely on fishing for household income. This means the intermediate-risk decision space is genuinely unoccupied. Fishers lack an operational framework for responding to moderate conditions with a graduated answer — modified area, shorter duration, nearshore restriction. Between obvious safety and obvious danger lies a range of conditions where graduated governance would enable continued, scope-restricted participation rather than a forced binary choice. The following section compares the proposed architecture against existing approaches to identify where this capability does not exist.

## 2.9 Comparative Analysis

The preceding sections have surveyed six bodies of literature across the governance problem: how AI decision support should behave in safety-critical environments. Each body addresses part of the problem. Evaluating them at the architectural level requires a shared analytical framework. This analysis uses four governance dimensions. The first is Level 1 governance — whether the system has a mechanism that controls when AI is allowed to participate. The second is Level 2 governance — whether the system has a mechanism that controls what AI is permitted to recommend. The third is unified governance — whether Level 1 and Level 2 operate as a coordinated pair conditioned on the same classified state variable. The fourth is environmental state conditioning — whether the governance trigger is classified environmental safety state, a function of observable operational conditions, rather than AI performance, output content, or design-time configuration. No existing system in the reviewed corpus satisfies all four dimensions.

But the tier is conditioned on verified agent robustness — an offline-assessed measure of AI trustworthiness — not on classified environmental state. Baxi's governance answers "how much can this AI be trusted?" The proposed architecture answers "what is AI permitted to recommend given current conditions?" These are orthogonal questions with different conditioning variables. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) document a three-level ODD → ROD → MRC degradation hierarchy for automated driving, where a Restricted Operational Domain narrows the vehicle's geographic scope before a minimum-risk condition stop. This is operational domain confinement — restricting where the system operates — not advisory type restriction within a fixed operational scope.

(2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) provide the definitive taxonomy: 13 guardrail action types across 14 pipeline targets. Both systems constrain what AI produces. But the constraint is fixed: the same rules apply regardless of operational conditions. A deteriorating sea state, an approaching nightfall, a recent marine warning — none changes the guardrail configuration. The prohibited output set is conditioned on output content, not on classified environmental safety state. This is static Level 2 governance. The mechanism that would narrow the permitted recommendation space as conditions deteriorate — and widen it as conditions improve — does not exist.

The pattern is consistent across all three categories. Safety gating addresses Level 1 without Level 2. Guardrails address Level 2 without environmental state conditioning. Adaptive autonomy adjusts monitoring intensity or operational domain without restricting advisory scope as a function of classified environmental state. No architecture implements G(S) and A_AI(S) as a unified pair conditioned on the same S = f(E). Four independent systematic reviews reach this conclusion from different bodies of literature. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) review 91 collaborative intelligence papers across six industries and confirm binary governance as universal. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) survey safety techniques across automotive, avionics, railway, and industrial domains and find no architecture restricts AI recommendation types based on classified safety state. Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) review 46 formal methods studies and find every method implements binary safety boundaries. At the institutional level, Attard-Frost & Lyons (2025) [[notes]](../notes/AI%20governance%20systems-%20A%20multi-scale%20analysis%20framework%2C%20empirical%20findings%2C%20and%20future%20directions.%20AI%20and%20Ethics.md) map 610 governance topics in a national AI governance system and find none references runtime environmental state classification, state-conditioned advisory scope restriction, or any form of graduated CAUTION mode. The gap is not an artefact of one literature stream. It is structural — present at the technical, formal methods, and institutional governance layers simultaneously. The comparative analysis confirms the gap. The following two sections consolidate the findings and state the research question it generates.

## 2.10 Literature Summary

The eight bodies of literature reviewed — AI decision support, safety gating, human-AI authority, adaptive autonomy, guardrails, hybrid AI, low-resource AI, and fisheries decision context — have collectively mapped the governance problem from different angles. The technical bodies have matured specific governance dimensions: safety gating delivers formally verified participation control; guardrails deliver rich output-restriction toolkits with 13 documented action types; adaptive autonomy demonstrates that governance posture need not be static; human-AI authority frameworks establish that oversight allocation is a distinct question from output restriction. These are genuine contributions. But each addresses one governance dimension in isolation. Safety gating controls when AI acts without restricting what it recommends. Guardrails restrict what AI recommends without conditioning on operational state. Adaptive autonomy varies monitoring intensity without restricting advisory scope. The enabling bodies — hybrid AI, low-resource AI, and the fisheries domain — establish that the gap has direct deployment consequences. In resource-constrained, safety-critical environments, binary governance transfers decision burden to humans who are less equipped to bear it. The intermediate-risk conditions where graduated guidance would matter most are precisely the conditions where no governance-aware AI decision support currently exists.

The collective limitation is binary governance — AI either acts or is blocked, with no intermediate mode in which it participates within a formally restricted advisory scope. This is confirmed at every level of analysis examined in this review. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) find binary governance universal across 91 safety-critical collaborative intelligence papers spanning six industries. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) find it universal across 11 international frontier AI safety frameworks developed by the world's leading AI organisations. Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) find it universal across 46 formal methods studies. The architecture required to address this gap — a governance pair (G(S), A_AI(S)) conditioned on classified environmental safety state S = f(E), with formal safety properties proved across that conditioning — does not appear in any reviewed body of literature. This is the research gap the following section states formally.

## 2.11 Research Gap

No existing architecture defines a two-level governance pair in which a participation gate G(S) and an admissible recommendation space A_AI(S) are jointly conditioned on the same classified environmental safety state S. No system classifies observable environmental conditions into a tripartite state S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE} using a deterministic function S = f(E) over a formally specified parameter vector. No system implements the containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ — ensuring that what AI may recommend narrows as environmental danger increases and reduces to the empty set in the most dangerous state. No system proves the Safety Dominance Property: AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S) for all environmental states E. Most critically, no system implements the CAUTION mode — the intermediate operational state in which AI participates in decision support, but only within a formally restricted advisory scope, because conditions are elevated but not yet prohibitive. Every existing mechanism treats AI participation as binary: the system either operates at full advisory scope or does not operate at all.

Binary governance forces a false dilemma. Full restriction — blocking AI when conditions are elevated — wastes a scarce decision support resource and transfers the reasoning burden to a human who, in low-resource environments, may be less equipped to bear it. Full permission — allowing AI to operate at unchanged advisory scope as conditions deteriorate — violates the principle that what AI should recommend depends on how dangerous the situation is. The CAUTION mode resolves this dilemma: AI continues providing decision support when conditions are elevated but not prohibitive, within a restricted advisory scope covering recommendation types that remain appropriate under those conditions — nearshore areas rather than open-water routes, shorter durations, weather-conditional itineraries. This is not a marginal refinement of existing approaches. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) implement three operational levels, but their intermediate level governs supervisory monitoring intensity, not advisory scope. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) implements nested permission sets with the right containment structure, but conditions on agent robustness — a static, offline-assessed property — not on classified environmental state. Neither implements a mode in which an AI decision support system participates under reduced advisory scope because the environment, not the AI, is in an elevated risk condition.

Three research gaps follow from this analysis. The first is architectural: no formally defined mechanism unifies participation governance and advisory scope governance under the same classified environmental state, with proved safety properties that hold across all conditions. The second is evaluative: no study compares a graduated two-level governance architecture against binary governance in a safety-critical deployment context — establishing whether graduated governance produces better decision outcomes without compromising safety.
