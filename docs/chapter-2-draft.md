# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 AI Decision Support in Safety-Critical Systems

Artificial intelligence is increasingly used to support human decision-making in safety-critical industries. Applications span automotive, avionics, railway, industrial robotics, and maritime domains, where AI systems provide predictions, risk assessments, and operational recommendations (Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md)). Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), in a systematic review of 91 studies on collaborative intelligence, identify two primary interaction modes: machine-assists-human and human-assists-machine. In both cases, AI functions in an advisory capacity. It augments human judgement rather than replacing it, because the complexity and consequence of safety-critical decisions still require human oversight. The International AI Safety Report notes that general-purpose AI capabilities are expanding rapidly, with systems now performing reasoning, planning, and autonomous action at scales not anticipated even two years prior (Bengio et al., 2026 [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md)). As a result, the scope and reach of AI advisory systems in safety-critical domains is growing — and with it, the consequences of getting AI behaviour wrong.

The benefits are well documented. AI reduces cognitive load by processing multiple data streams simultaneously. It identifies patterns across larger datasets than humans can evaluate (Ramos et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md)). In practice, it also improves situational awareness in time-pressured environments — this happens because it can integrate heterogeneous inputs, such as sensor readings, forecasts, and historical records, faster than a human operator. Bloomfield and Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) make a stronger claim: AI enables functional capabilities, such as real-time perception and dynamic risk estimation, that conventional software simply cannot replicate. Bengio, Hinton et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Managing%20extreme%20AI%20risks%20amid%20rapid%20progress.md), in a consensus statement by 25 leading AI researchers, acknowledge that AI has produced substantial benefits across scientific research, healthcare, and engineering. None of this is disputed.

The problem is that AI systems also produce incorrect outputs — and in safety-critical environments, incorrect recommendations do not simply degrade performance. They anchor human decisions toward unsafe outcomes. Wen et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Risk%20Perception%20in%20Complex%20Systems-%20A%20Comparative%20Analysis%20of%20Process%20Control%20and%20Autonomous%20Vehicle%20Failures.md) demonstrate this empirically. Analysing 60 real-world accident reports across process control and autonomous vehicle domains, they find that human intervention was ineffective in 83.3% of process control cases and 66.7% of autonomous vehicle cases. What is striking is that the dominant failure mode was not technical malfunction. It was human–automation interaction breakdown: over-reliance, mode confusion, passive monitoring. This shows that the risk lies not only in the AI output itself but in how humans respond to it. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) identify a related concern from the formal methods side. Reviewing 46 studies, they observe that nearly all safety enforcement is applied reactively — after AI produces output — rather than proactively restricting what AI is permitted to produce. Bloomfield and Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) put it plainly: AI and ML components are opaque, experimentally derived, and cannot satisfy classical fault-avoidance assurance requirements. In other words, when AI operates in safety-critical contexts, the system must account not only for what AI gets right but for what happens when it gets things wrong. This raises a deeper question — not only how to verify AI outputs, but whether AI should be allowed to produce them under all conditions.

Current research addresses this challenge primarily through two strategies: improving AI accuracy and improving AI explainability. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), surveying safety techniques across industrial and transportation domains, identify safety bags, safety monitors, and safety envelopes as the dominant runtime mechanisms. All three verify or filter AI outputs after generation. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) confirm this pattern across a broader scope: they survey eight categories of formal methods — reachability, SMT verification, model checking, runtime verification, shielding, control barrier functions, risk verification, and MILP/ILP — and find that none governs AI participation or advisory scope as a function of classified operational conditions. This means that the field has focused on making AI outputs safer or more transparent, but has not asked a more fundamental question: under what conditions should AI participate in decision-making at all? And when it does participate, what should it be permitted to recommend? The governance of AI behaviour under varying safety conditions — not just the correctness of AI outputs — is the central concern of this review.

## 2.2 Deterministic Safety Constraints and Safety Gating

The literature's first response to the problem of unsafe AI behaviour has been deterministic safety constraints. These mechanisms sit alongside or around the AI system and enforce safety properties that the AI itself cannot guarantee. Three architectural patterns dominate. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) formalise *shields* — runtime mechanisms synthesised from LTL safety specifications and environment models that intercept unsafe actions before they reach the environment. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) propose Guaranteed Safe AI, a framework built on three components: a world model, a safety specification, and a verifier that produces auditable proof certificates before AI output is deployed. Bajcsy and Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) take a control-theoretic approach, defining a Human-AI Safety Filter that monitors AI outputs and intervenes at the last possible moment to prevent trajectory-level safety violations. What these approaches share is a common structural principle: a deterministic layer constrains a probabilistic AI layer, because the AI cannot be trusted to self-regulate.

The mechanism across all three patterns is a binary gate. The shield either permits the agent's action or replaces it with a safe alternative (Könighofer et al., 2025 [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md)). The verifier either certifies the output or blocks deployment (Dalrymple et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md)). The safety filter either lets the action pass or overrides it with a fallback policy (Bajcsy & Fisac, 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md)). More recent work refines this pattern but does not change its binary character. Corsi et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Verification-Guided%20Shielding%20for%20Deep%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) use formal DNN verification to partition the input space into provably safe and potentially unsafe regions, activating the shield only where needed — reducing overhead by 25–71%, but the shield itself still operates as a binary allow-or-replace gate. Abella et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/SAFEXPLAIN-%20a%20Complete%20Approach%20Towards%20Trustworthy%20AI-Based%20Safety-Critical%20Systems.md), in the EU SAFEXPLAIN project, implement a supervision function that bounds AI operations within a predefined safety envelope and can switch to a non-AI fallback subsystem. Vermaelen and Holvoet (2025) [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md) use constraint-based planning with state rules that exclude any action whose foreseeable outcomes violate safety conditions. In every case, the governance decision is binary: safe or unsafe, permit or block, continue or fall back. There is no intermediate mode.

This binary pattern is not an artefact of a few selected papers. It holds across the literature at scale. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), reviewing 91 collaborative intelligence studies across safety-critical industries, find no system that varies its advisory scope based on classified safety state. Newcomb and Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) survey 46 formal methods studies and identify shielding, runtime verification, and control barrier functions as the dominant enforcement mechanisms — all binary. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), mapping 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks, confirm that risk tiering triggers binary deployment decisions: capability thresholds either permit or restrict AI deployment, with no graduated advisory scope. Odriozola-Olalde et al. (2023) [[notes]](../notes/Shielded%20Reinforcement%20Learning-%20A%20review%20of%20reactive%20methods%20for%20safe%20learning.md) conduct a dedicated review of all shielded reinforcement learning methods and classify them into three Safety Levels — but all three levels implement binary per-action gates. No reviewed method restricts the *types* of actions an agent may propose as a function of environmental state. The absence of an intermediate governance mode is consistent and cross-domain.

These safety constraints address an important question — whether AI should act — but they leave a second question unanswered: what should AI be allowed to recommend when it does act? Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) classify AI safety compliance into three classes, where Class II systems use a certified safety bag to monitor an uncertified AI item. Even in this most structured pattern, the safety bag checks whether AI output is safe. It does not restrict the scope of what the AI is permitted to produce in the first place. In the terminology of this review, existing safety constraints implement Level 1 governance — they control AI participation — but provide no Level 2 governance over advisory scope. If the gate is open, the AI operates at full capability regardless of risk conditions. A separate question therefore arises: how should decision authority be allocated between human and AI, and does that allocation solve the advisory scope problem?
