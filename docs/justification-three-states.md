# Why Three States? Justification for SAFE / CAUTION / UNSAFE

**Document type**: Theoretical justification
**For**: Chapter 2 and Chapter 3
**Question**: Why three discrete safety states rather than: continuous risk, 5+ levels, binary, or probabilistic threshold?

---

## 1. The Core Claim

The proposed architecture classifies environmental conditions into S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE} using the function S = f(E), where E is a vector of observable environmental parameters. The governance pair (G(S), A_AI(S)) maps each state to AI participation and advisory scope, with the containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ enforced as a formal invariant.

Three discrete states are not an arbitrary design choice. Three is the *minimum number of states needed to resolve the binary false dilemma* — the forced choice between over-restriction (block all AI under any uncertainty) and under-restriction (permit full AI advisory scope regardless of conditions) — while remaining operationally tractable, formally tractable, and empirically grounded in domain practice. Each alternative is examined and rejected on principled grounds below.

---

## 2. Why Not Binary?

The dominant paradigm across safety-critical AI governance is binary: AI either operates fully or is blocked entirely. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), in a systematic review of 91 papers on collaborative intelligence for safety-critical industries, found that all safety governance mechanisms across those papers are binary — Level 1 governance (whether AI participates) is the universal form, and no paper implements graduated advisory scope restriction. At the largest institutional scale, Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) document that all 11 major Frontier AI Safety Frameworks reviewed — including those of Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, NVIDIA, and Cohere — implement binary participation control at the point of deployment decisions: AI is deployed (with uniform safeguards applied regardless of operational state) or it is not deployed. As the report states, the dominant paradigm is one in which "AI either operates fully or is blocked entirely."

Binary governance forces a false dilemma that is ecologically invalid in coastal fisheries. Empirical research on small-scale fisher decision-making in Penang documents a tripartite, not binary, behavioural pattern: conditions are classified as go, cautious-go, or don't-go (Gao, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md). This informal graduated classification is not an abstract model — it is documented as the actual decision logic fishers employ, with weather (importance rating 3.75), tide (4.55), and safety concern (3.40) operating as the primary gatekeepers. Adverse weather "can halt fishing trips altogether" (UNSAFE), while marginal conditions produce a documented intermediate regime where fishers "go with caution" — shortening trips, moving to near-shore locations, and actively sharing weather information through social networks (Gao, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md). The binary model has no mechanism to represent or support this intermediate state.

Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md), studying 79 small-scale fishing households in coastal Makassar City, Indonesia, document the same tripartite structure empirically through seasonal classification: the West season (intense winds 30–40 knots, waves exceeding 2 metres) functions as a natural UNSAFE analogue; the Fishing season (low winds, mild swells) functions as a SAFE analogue; and the East season — with vigorous winds but calm seas — represents a mixed condition that is neither safe nor dangerous and maps precisely onto the CAUTION analogue. Critically, Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) also document that fishers routinely *ignore* binary governmental warnings from Indonesia's Ministry of Maritime Affairs (KKP) because a binary "don't sail" instruction fails to accommodate the adaptive near-shore, restricted-duration fishing that fishers already practise under marginal conditions. The CAUTION mode — a restricted-but-non-empty advisory space — is the mechanism the binary paradigm structurally cannot provide.

The most current primary study of the exact target population — Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md), surveying 136 small-scale fishers in central Terengganu, Malaysia (published in *Frontiers in Marine Science*, Q1) — provides the strongest quantitative evidence yet for both the binary status quo and its inadequacy. The binary decision pattern is confirmed by the empirical knowledge item: "I know if it's suddenly windy/change of weather, I can't go fishing" — agreed by the majority of respondents, with no intermediate response option documented in the questionnaire or observed in practice. Flexibility is identified as the weakest of five adaptive capacity domains: only 58% of fishers consider alternative livelihoods and 67.6% rely solely on fishing for household income. The five-domain assessment (assets, flexibility, social organisation, learning, agency) contains no item capturing intermediate-risk behaviour modification under elevated but non-extreme conditions. Critically, this binary pattern is not merely a decision preference — it is a measured *adaptive capacity deficit*. The CAUTION mode is therefore not simply a new governance mode; it is a decision support mechanism that addresses a population-level capability gap that Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) document empirically.

Earlier background work — Shaffril et al. (2017) [[notes]](../notes/Adapting%20towards%20climate%20change%20impacts-%20Strategies%20for%20small-scale%20fishermen%20in%20Malaysia%20.md), studying Malaysian small-scale fishermen — documented that fishermen operating vessels ≤22 feet responded to environmental risk with binary cessation: during the north-east monsoon, operating days were reduced by up to 90% and boat rental services to islands were "temporarily suspended." No intermediate strategy was recorded in which fishermen modified *what they do* under elevated-but-non-extreme risk. While nearly a decade has passed since this study and the Malaysian fishing sector has changed, it provides historical background supporting the observation that the *cautious-go* intermediate had no formal governance analogue in Malaysian fisheries at the time of the study. Shaffril et al. (2017) [[notes]](../notes/Adapting%20towards%20climate%20change%20impacts-%20Strategies%20for%20small-scale%20fishermen%20in%20Malaysia%20.md) is cited here for historical contextual grounding; the primary current evidence for the binary status quo in the exact target population is Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md).

Binary governance also produces two forms of calibration failure. Overtrust (following AI recommendations uncritically when conditions are degraded) leads to unsafe decisions. Undertrust (rejecting all AI participation whenever any uncertainty exists) wastes the system's decision-support capacity and may increase economic pressure on fishers to make uninformed decisions alone. McGrath et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) demonstrate that these calibration failures — "overreliance and lack of monitoring" when trust exceeds optimal, and "disuse of technological systems" when trust falls below optimal — are the two failure modes that any decision-support architecture must design against. Three states, not two, provide the intermediate calibration point that prevents both failure modes simultaneously.

The formal case against binary constraint is strengthened by Banerjee et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/CRANE-%20Reasoning%20with%20Constrained%20LLM%20Generation.md), who prove via circuit complexity (Proposition 3.1) that restricting LLM outputs to a grammar encoding only valid final answers collapses the model's problem-solving capability to TC⁰ — a restrictive complexity class insufficient for problems requiring intermediate reasoning chains. Their solution — an augmented grammar G_a = R_M · G permitting reasoning tokens followed by constrained output — restores full expressivity. This is the formal analogue of the three-state argument: binary restriction (full constraint or none) is computationally suboptimal; an intermediate mode (restricted-but-non-empty scope) preserves capability. While CRANE operates at the token-generation level with syntactic triggers rather than at the governance level with environmental state conditioning, the theoretical result provides independent computational-theoretic motivation for why a CAUTION mode — restricted advisory scope that preserves reasoning capability — is architecturally superior to a binary choice between full scope and no scope.

## 3. Why Not Continuous Risk Value?

A continuous risk variable — a real-valued score R ∈ [0, 1] — might appear to offer finer resolution than three discrete states. This alternative fails on three independent grounds: cognitive tractability, governance tractability, and formal tractability.

**Cognitive tractability.** A continuous risk output requires operators to interpret a numerical score and determine — in real time, under operational stress — what action to take. Wen et al. (2025), in a comparative analysis of 60 real-world accident reports across process control and autonomous vehicle domains, find that human intervention was ineffective in 83.3% of process control system failures and 66.7% of autonomous vehicle failures. They attribute this to cognitive overload, passive monitoring, and mode confusion — conditions that are exacerbated, not resolved, by presenting operators with undifferentiated probabilistic outputs. Their recommendation is for frameworks that integrate "deterministic safety logic with adaptive AI intelligence" — precisely the pattern implemented by a discrete, classified state that enables deterministic governance. An operator reading "R = 0.62" has no immediate, unambiguous mapping to action. An operator reading "CAUTION" does.

**Governance tractability.** The governance functions G(S) and A_AI(S) require S to be a discrete variable with well-defined per-state semantics. G(S): {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE} → {0, 1} is a well-defined function. G(R): [0, 1] → {0, 1} requires a threshold — and any threshold choice collapses the continuous variable into a binary or at best binary-with-hysteresis classification. More critically, A_AI(S) — the admissible advisory space per state — is defined over discrete recommendation *types* (departure time, fishing ground, species selection, trip duration). There is no meaningful analogue to A_AI(0.62): you cannot define a continuous variation of which recommendation *categories* the AI is permitted to generate. Categorical governance requires categorical states.

**Maritime domain practice.** Operational risk classification in maritime systems is categorical, not continuous. Madsen & Kim (2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md), reviewing AI decision transparency for autonomous shipping, document that the dominant visualisation paradigm for navigational risk is colour-coded categorical classification — green, amber, and red warning tiers that map environmental traffic conditions to discrete risk categories. They note that systems by Ożoga & Montewka (2018) classify into five colour bands from "no hazard" to "extreme danger" based on TCPA/CPA parameters, and systems reviewed by Van de Merwe et al. (2023) use traffic-light colour coding — both categorical, not continuous. The categorical approach is adopted specifically because operators cannot reliably act on raw numerical risk values in time-pressured maritime navigation decisions.

**Formal tractability.** The Safety Dominance Property AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S) and the containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ require S to be a discrete variable with well-defined, verifiable per-state recommendation spaces. Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md), in a systematic review of formal methods for safety-critical machine learning covering 46 studies, document that all formal verification approaches — including shielding, runtime verification, control barrier functions, and model checking — operate with binary safety boundaries: safe/unsafe, within-bounds/out-of-bounds, property-satisfied/property-violated. A continuous variable S cannot support a formal containment hierarchy because the containment statement A_AI(s₁) ⊃ A_AI(s₂) requires s₁ and s₂ to be discrete, named states — you cannot prove A_AI(0.73) ⊃ A_AI(0.45) with meaningful categorical content.

## 4. Why Not Five or More Levels?

If binary is too coarse and continuous is too fine, why not five levels? The case against five or more levels rests on the principle of minimum viable graduated governance: more levels require more transition thresholds, more governance modes, more advisory scope boundaries to define formally, and more cognitive burden for operators navigating mode transitions.

**Cognitive load.** Wen et al. (2025) document that mode confusion — a form of cognitive failure in which operators misidentify which operational mode the system is in — was a recurrent cause of failure across both process control (13.3% of accident cases) and autonomous vehicle domains (10.0% of accident cases). Every additional discrete level is an additional mode that operators must track, distinguish, and respond to correctly under time pressure. Three levels define two transition boundaries (SAFE→CAUTION and CAUTION→UNSAFE), each with a clear directional change in AI advisory scope. Five levels define four boundaries, each requiring operationally distinct responses.

**Trust calibration cost.** McGrath et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) establish that trust in AI systems requires active recalibration at each mode transition — expectations built in one governance mode carry forward as miscalibrated trust unless explicitly corrected at the transition point. Each additional level multiplies the recalibration burden. Trust calibration requires stable, distinguishable system modes; five subtly differentiated levels produce modes that operators cannot reliably distinguish, collapsing effective governance back toward binary.

**Domain practice does not support five levels.** The comparative evidence across safety-critical domains consistently supports three, not five. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md), reviewing safety evidence methods for automated driving systems, document the canonical graduated degradation hierarchy used in the automotive domain: the Operational Design Domain (ODD) — full capability — degrading to the Restricted Operational Domain (ROD) — reduced capability — and finally to the Minimal Risk Condition (MRC) — the fail-safe minimum. This is an independently derived three-level hierarchy from a mature safety-critical domain. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md), extending the ISO/IEC TR 5469 framework for AI in safety-critical systems, propose a traffic-light degradation index — Level 1 (green), Level 2 (orange), Level 3 (red) — as a three-level classification for AI quality assurance. The authors explicitly state this is "the first framework or method for indexing AI degradation in safety-critical systems in such a manner," and they select three levels specifically because this is the minimum number that provides an actionable intermediate state distinguishable from both full operation and shutdown. Neither paper proposes five levels.

**Minimum complexity for maximum tractability.** Three states satisfy the formal containment property with minimum complexity: SAFE = full advisory scope, UNSAFE = empty advisory scope, CAUTION = the novel intermediate state where AI participates under scope restriction. Adding a fourth or fifth state requires defining additional advisory scope boundaries — what is permitted at level 3 that is not permitted at level 4, and how is that boundary formally specified and verified? Three states achieve the governance objective with the fewest formal obligations and the lowest operational cognitive load.

## 5. Why Not Probabilistic Threshold?

A probabilistic threshold model — where governance decisions are triggered when P(unsafe) exceeds some value θ — appears to offer principled grounding in uncertainty quantification. This alternative fails on three grounds: calibration impossibility in data-scarce fisheries, the inability to produce discrete governance modes, and the incompatibility with how human operators actually process and respond to communicated system states.

**Calibration impossibility.** Probabilistic thresholds require reliable probability estimates of environmental state. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) document that small-scale Indonesian coastal fishers have no access to current meteorological data and rely entirely on personal experience and natural indicators (sea wind direction, water temperature, marine organisms, ocean currents) to assess conditions. The environmental data infrastructure required to produce calibrated P(unsafe) estimates does not exist in this deployment context. An uncalibrated probability is not a probability — it is an arbitrary scalar that introduces false precision without providing decision support.

**No discrete governance modes.** A probabilistic threshold model produces a binary governance decision at each threshold crossing — which does not differ structurally from binary governance except that the switching point is probabilistic rather than deterministic. The CAUTION mode cannot be derived from a probabilistic threshold: CAUTION is not "P(unsafe) is rising" but a structurally distinct governance mode with a formally defined advisory scope restriction A_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A_AI(SAFE). Probabilistic thresholds produce continuous risk monitoring, not categorical governance.

**Human operators cannot act on probability values.** Schrills et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Questioning%20Trust%20in%20AI%20Research-%20Exploring%20the%20Influence%20of%20Trust%20Assessment%20on%20Dependence%20in%20AI-Assisted%20Decision-Making.md), in a preregistered experiment on trust and dependence in AI-assisted decision-making (N = 149), find that "instructed reliability appears to be more influential in determining behaviour compared to assessed trust." Participants matched their dependence on AI recommendations to the *communicated* reliability level with striking consistency — a probability matching effect. The implication is that what operators are *told about the system's state* shapes their behaviour more powerfully than their subjective trust attitude or the actual statistical reliability. Communicating "P(unsafe) = 0.62" provides no actionable anchor — it is not a reliability level operators can match their behaviour to. Communicating "CAUTION mode: restricted advisory scope" provides the discrete, stable state description that anchors appropriate reliance behaviour.

**Trust calibration requires stable discrete states.** McGrath et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) establish that trust calibration — the alignment of operator expectations with actual system capabilities — requires stable, predictable system modes. "Each of these applications requires its own 'recipe' for trust, and each of these recipes may work best with particular ingredients." A probabilistic threshold model produces continuously shifting governance states, making stable trust calibration impossible. The SAFE/CAUTION/UNSAFE classification provides three stable, predictable, and semantically distinct states, each requiring a different calibrated trust "recipe" that operators can reliably develop through repeated exposure.

**Interpretability under operational conditions.** Atf & Lewis (2026), in a meta-analysis of 90 studies on the explainability-trust relationship (r = 0.194, p < 0.001), find that the correlation between AI interpretability and user trust is statistically significant but weak. Their central argument is that interpretable outputs enable users to determine *whether* to trust, not simply to trust more — supporting the principle that governance state communication must provide clear categorical anchors, not probabilistic outputs that users must themselves interpret. A probabilistic threshold requires operators to perform their own interpretation of a numerical estimate before they can form any governance judgement. A discrete state label performs this interpretation within the system and presents the result in a directly actionable form.

## 6. Why THREE Specifically?

The preceding sections establish why alternatives to three discrete states fail. This section provides the positive argument: three is not merely the best of available alternatives but the structurally correct answer for this architecture.

**Three is the minimum number of states needed to resolve the binary false dilemma.** SAFE maps to full AI participation (G(S) = 1, A_AI = full), UNSAFE maps to no AI participation (G(S) = 0, A_AI = ∅), and CAUTION is the novel intermediate state that binary governance cannot represent: G(S) = 1 but A_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A_AI(SAFE). CAUTION is not merely a "softer off" or "stronger on" — it is a structurally distinct governance mode where AI participates but with a formally constrained recommendation space. Any architecture with fewer than three states cannot represent this intermediate mode. Any architecture with more than three states multiplies governance obligations without adding structural distinctiveness.

**Three is empirically validated across multiple independent lines of evidence in the target domain.** Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) documents the tripartite go/cautious-go/don't-go classification used by Penang small-scale fishers — empirically observed from 25 semi-structured interviews, not theoretically imposed. The classification drives different scopes of decision-making: under favourable conditions, fishers decide timing, location, species, and duration; under marginal conditions, the scope collapses to location selection and trip-shortening considerations; under adverse conditions, no operational decisions are made because the trip is cancelled. This is an empirical observation of A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ as it exists in human decision-making prior to any AI system. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) independently document the three-season structure (Fishing/East/West) that maps onto the same tripartite classification from a different methodological approach (quantitative survey, logistic regression, SWOT analysis) in a different national context (Indonesia vs. Malaysia). The convergence across independent empirical studies in the domain provides strong evidence that three states, not two or five, reflects the actual structure of environmental risk as experienced and acted upon by the target users.

**Three maps to the canonical safety engineering response hierarchy.** The proceed / proceed with caution / stop hierarchy is the foundational behavioural grammar of safety-critical decision-making across domains. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) document ODD → ROD → MRC as this hierarchy's formal expression in automotive safety; Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) independently derive green → orange → red in quality assurance for AI-enabled safety-critical systems; Madsen & Kim (2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md) document that maritime collision avoidance systems use colour-coded categorical risk classifications that map to the same proceed/caution/stop logic. Three-level classification is not a design choice specific to this architecture — it is the independently recurring solution to the problem of graduated response in safety-critical operational contexts.

**Three satisfies the formal containment property with minimum complexity.** The containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ requires at least three distinct sets — the property is trivially satisfied (and trivially useless) with two states, where SAFE ⊃ UNSAFE = ∅. The third state (CAUTION) is the smallest possible addition that produces a non-trivial, operationally useful containment hierarchy. Baxi (2026) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md), in a formally rigorous architecture for AI economic governance, independently derives the minimum viable K-tier system as one with K ≥ 3, proving that a single intermediate tier is required to achieve the formal property that "high scores on one dimension must not compensate for failures on another" — the structural non-compensability principle that the CAUTION mode instantiates. Their containment relationship T₁ ⊂ T₂ ⊂ ... ⊂ T_K mirrors A_AI(UNSAFE) ⊂ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A_AI(SAFE) in both structure and proof logic.

## 7. Formal Properties Require Discrete States

The Safety Dominance Property AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S) and the containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ require S to be a discrete variable with well-defined per-state recommendation spaces.

A continuous variable S cannot support a formal containment hierarchy. The statement A_AI(0.73) ⊃ A_AI(0.45) would require: (a) defining what types of recommendations are admissible at score 0.73 but not at 0.45, (b) proving that this relationship holds for all valid inputs E that produce those scores, and (c) verifying at runtime that a specific recommendation type falls in or outside the admissible set for a given score. None of these steps are computationally tractable without first discretising the score — at which point the continuous variable has become a discrete classification in disguise.

Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) synthesise the state of formal methods for safety-critical machine learning and find that all formal verification approaches — shielding, runtime verification, control barrier functions, SMT-based verification, model checking — operate with binary safety boundaries (safe set / unsafe set, property satisfied / property violated). This is not an accident of domain convention but a consequence of the structure of formal proofs: you can prove that an action belongs to a well-defined set; you cannot prove that it belongs to a point on a continuous scale without discretising that scale first. The formal containment property this architecture requires is only expressible over discrete states.

Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md), formalising shields for safe reinforcement learning, demonstrate that formal safety guarantees require a precisely defined "winning region" — the set of all safe states from which the agent can always select a safe action regardless of environmental dynamics. This winning region is a discrete partition of the state space: a state is in the winning region or it is not. The shield's ability to provide formal safety guarantees derives directly from this discreteness. An analogous discreteness is required for the containment property: A_AI(CAUTION) must be a precisely defined set — not a fuzzy or probabilistically varying range — for the inclusion A_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A_AI(SAFE) to be formally provable and computationally verifiable at runtime.

Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), surveying AI for safety-critical systems in industrial and transportation domains, document that the Class I / Class II / Class III taxonomy for safety-critical AI deployment is similarly discrete: a system is either in a class that admits formal verification (I), a class that requires compensatory safety bags (II), or a class where AI is inadmissible in safety functions (III). The discreteness of this classification is not incidental — it reflects the requirement that safety assurance arguments must have binary, verifiable conclusions. Graduated governance over recommendation scope demands the same discreteness.

## 9. One-Paragraph Summary

The architecture classifies environmental conditions into three discrete safety states — SAFE, CAUTION, and UNSAFE — because three is the minimum number of states needed to resolve the binary governance false dilemma while remaining formally tractable, operationally interpretable, and empirically grounded in the target domain. Binary governance, the universal paradigm across 91 collaborative intelligence papers (Ramos et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) and 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks (Bengio et al., 2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), cannot represent the intermediate operational mode that both domain evidence and formal requirements demand. Empirical research on small-scale fisher decision-making documents a tripartite go/cautious-go/don't-go classification that is independently observed in Penang (Gao, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) and Indonesia (Rahim et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md), with the intermediate mode characterised by restricted but non-empty operational scope — precisely the CAUTION mode's behavioural signature. Continuous risk values, five-level schemes, and probabilistic thresholds are rejected because: continuous values require numerical interpretation under operational stress that produces the cognitive failures documented by Wen et al. (2025); five-level schemes multiply governance obligations and mode-confusion risk beyond what domain practice supports (Gyllenhammar et al., 2025 [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md); Flehmig et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md)); and probabilistic thresholds require calibration data absent from small-scale fisheries and cannot produce the stable, discrete system states that are necessary for trust calibration (McGrath et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) and that users demonstrably rely upon as dependence anchors (Schrills et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Questioning%20Trust%20in%20AI%20Research-%20Exploring%20the%20Influence%20of%20Trust%20Assessment%20on%20Dependence%20in%20AI-Assisted%20Decision-Making.md). Three states satisfy the formal containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ with minimum complexity (Newcomb & Ochoa, 2026 [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md); Könighofer et al., 2025 [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md); Baxi, 2026 [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md)), align with the canonical proceed/caution/stop response hierarchy found in traffic-light safety indexing (Flehmig et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md), automotive degradation hierarchies (Gyllenhammar et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md), and maritime warning classification (Madsen & Kim, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/A%20state-of-the-art%20review%20of%20AI%20decision%20transparency%20for%20autonomous%20shipping.md), and are interpretable under operational conditions without requiring operators to interpret numerical risk outputs — a design requirement underscored by the low explainability-trust correlation (r = 0.194) found across 90 studies (Atf & Lewis, 2026) and by Saup et al.'s (2026) finding that categorical decision categories are necessary for AI governance to produce actionable organisational decision inputs.
