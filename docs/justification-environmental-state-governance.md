# Why Environmental Safety State? Justification for Environment-Driven Governance

**Document type**: Theoretical justification / argumentation note  
**For**: Chapter 2 (Literature Review) and Chapter 3 (Architecture Design)  
**Question addressed**: Why is governance conditioned on *environmental safety state* rather than AI confidence, risk score, model uncertainty, human workload, cost function, reward function, or performance degradation?

---

## 1. The Core Claim

The proposed architecture conditions both governance functions — AI participation G(S) and advisory scope A_AI(S) — on a classified **environmental safety state** S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE}, where S = f(E) and E is a vector of observable environmental parameters (wind speed, wave height, visibility, rainfall, vessel condition, time of day).

This is a deliberate design choice, not a default. Each alternative governance trigger below represents a legitimate research direction. The argument is not that the alternatives are wrong in general, but that environmental state is the *correct* trigger for this problem — a safety-critical, human-in-the-loop decision support system operating in dynamic maritime environments.

---

## 2. Alternative Triggers and Why Each Fails Here

### 2.1 AI Confidence

**What it is**: The model's own estimated probability or softmax score over its output — how certain the AI is about its recommendation.

**Why it fails as a governance trigger**:

1. **Self-referential**: Asking the AI to govern itself based on its own confidence is circular. A model can be confidently wrong — this is the well-documented overconfidence problem in deep neural networks, particularly under distribution shift. In fisheries, a model trained on calm-weather data may produce high confidence outputs under storm conditions precisely because the input falls into a familiar-but-mismatched pattern. Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) find that calibration of confidence measures in safety-critical ML is an active open problem — "uncertainty estimates are themselves unreliable in out-of-distribution scenarios" — confirming that AI confidence cannot serve as a trustworthy trigger. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) similarly document that even the most mature deployed safety-critical AI systems rely on external safety bags and monitors rather than on AI self-assessment, precisely because self-referential confidence cannot be certified (Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md), §3.4). Bloomfield & Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) make this point forcefully: "internal guardrails are driven from the same perception and local model as the main action system, providing no protection for faults in those elements, and it is difficult to argue that they are either independent or diverse."

2. **Not causally upstream**: Environmental conditions *cause* unsafe outcomes. AI confidence is downstream of environmental conditions — it reflects how well the model's training distribution matches the current input, not whether the environment is actually safe. The causal structure is:

   > **Environment → Safety risk → (both) AI input quality AND real-world danger**

   Governing on AI confidence captures only the first path, not the direct environmental risk.

3. **Not independently verifiable**: A fisher cannot verify "confidence = 0.73" against their own experience. Governance triggers in human-in-the-loop systems must be interpretable and independently assessable by the human operator. As Bach et al. (2024) document through a systematic review of 23 empirical studies, trust in AI systems depends centrally on users' ability to understand and relate to system rationale — opacity in governance triggers systematically undermines trust calibration.

4. **Unstable at operational timescale**: Confidence fluctuates with each input instance, potentially triggering governance changes multiple times within a single decision session. Environmental state changes at a physical timescale (hours for weather), providing stable governance periods aligned with actual operational decisions. McGrath et al. (2025, CHAI-T) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) identify predictability — "consistency or regularity of behaviour" — as a distinct trustworthiness dimension; a governance trigger that oscillates within a single decision session violates this requirement.

**Relevant evidence**: Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) find that calibration of confidence measures in safety-critical ML is an active open problem — uncertainty estimates are themselves unreliable in out-of-distribution scenarios. Bloomfield & Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) further observe that internal AI guardrails are unassurable because they share the same perception and model as the system they are supposed to govern.

### 2.2 Risk Score

**What it is**: A computed aggregate score combining multiple factors (environmental, operational, model quality) into a single numerical risk value.

**Why it fails as a governance trigger**:

1. **Derived, not primitive**: A risk score must be computed *from* something. If computed from environmental parameters, it is the same as environmental state governance with an extra abstraction layer that adds opacity without benefit. If computed from model outputs, it inherits all the problems of AI confidence above. Liang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md) implement a gatekeeper architecture in which safety specifications derived from a world model govern AI output, and demonstrate a direct accuracy-failure trade-off: at gatekeeper threshold τ = 0.7, the system achieved the highest BLEU score improvement (54% over baseline) but also the highest failure rate. Aggregated risk scores face the same trade-off without providing the formal coverage guarantee that conformal prediction offers.

2. **Non-transparent aggregation**: Combining heterogeneous factors into a single score via weighted sum or learned function hides which factor is driving governance. For a fisher to trust governance mode changes, they need to understand *why* the mode changed. "Risk score crossed 0.65" is uninterpretable; "winds exceeded 25 knots" is. Atf & Lewis (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Is%20Trust%20Correlated%20With%20Explainability%20in%20AI%3F%20A%20Meta-Analysis.md) find through meta-analysis of 90 studies that AI explainability has only a weak positive correlation with trust (r = 0.194), and that "trust empowerment" — providing users with enough information to calibrate their own trust — requires complete, interpretable information, not an opaque aggregate.

3. **Calibration problem**: Who calibrates the risk score? Risk scores require domain-expert calibration and continuous updating. In low-resource settings, maintaining a calibrated risk scoring model is itself a significant technical burden. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) note that shield models similarly depend on the correctness of the underlying environment model — "if the model fails to capture a safety-relevant aspect, the shield cannot avoid safety-critical behavior" — and a learned risk score inherits this dependence without offering the formal guarantees of a shield.

4. **Formal property difficulty**: Proving the Safety Dominance Property AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S) requires that S is a function of observable quantities with known relationships to physical safety. A learned risk score makes this formal argument dependent on the risk model's correctness — not a foundation for a safety proof. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) specify that a world model must provide a "mathematical description of how the AI system affects the outside world" against which safety specifications can be verified; a learned risk score introduces a circularity that undermines this verifiability requirement.

### 2.3 Model Uncertainty

**What it is**: Epistemic or aleatoric uncertainty estimated by the model — typically via Bayesian methods, Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or conformal prediction intervals.

**Why it fails as a governance trigger**:

1. **Uncertainty ≠ danger**: High model uncertainty means the model does not know — it does not mean the environment is dangerous. Low model uncertainty does not mean the environment is safe. The two are correlated but distinct. A model can have low uncertainty about an incorrect prediction. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) formalise this distinction precisely: a shield's "winning region" (states from which safety can be guaranteed) is defined by the environment's physical dynamics, not by the model's self-assessed uncertainty. Safety and uncertainty are separate properties.

2. **Computationally expensive**: Proper uncertainty quantification (deep ensembles, Bayesian inference) requires significant computational resources. In edge-deployed, low-resource settings (intermittent connectivity, limited compute), reliable uncertainty quantification may not be feasible in real-time. Liang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md) deploy conformal prediction as their principled uncertainty approach and acknowledge the resource cost; they also document that the binary pass/reject paradigm creates an accuracy-failure trade-off precisely because uncertainty is not the same as danger.

3. **Uncertainty estimates are themselves uncertain**: The reliability of uncertainty estimates depends on the quality of the underlying model and the coverage of the calibration dataset. In data-scarce fisheries environments, calibration is impossible. Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) and Bloomfield & Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) both identify this as a fundamental limitation of uncertainty-based AI assurance approaches — uncertainty is a symptom-level signal, not a root-cause indicator.

4. **Not interpretable to domain users**: Fishers cannot act on "epistemic uncertainty = 0.31, aleatoric = 0.18." They can act on "sea state: CAUTION — waves 1.5–3m." Aquilino et al. (2025) establish that for low-anthropomorphism, functional decision support tools, cognitive trust — grounded in evaluation of reliability and competence, not technical uncertainty metrics — is the dominant trust component. An uncertainty-based trigger presents exactly the kind of technical concept that fails to activate cognitive trust for domain practitioners.

**Relevant evidence**: Liang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md) use conformal prediction to provide coverage guarantees — a principled uncertainty approach — but acknowledge the binary pass/reject paradigm creates an accuracy-failure trade-off precisely because uncertainty is not the same as danger.

### 2.4 Human Workload

**What it is**: The cognitive or physical load on the human operator, measured physiologically (EEG, eye-tracking, heart rate) or via proxy (task count, response latency).

**Why it fails as a governance trigger**:

1. **Wrong causality direction**: Human workload is a *consequence* of environmental conditions, not a cause of risk. Governing AI based on human workload treats the symptom (overloaded human) not the source (dangerous environment). Reducing AI scope when the human is overloaded is the opposite of what is needed — that is when AI assistance is most valuable. Wen et al. (2025) document through analysis of 60 real-world accident reports that human intervention was ineffective in 83.3% of process control system accidents and 66.7% of autonomous vehicle accidents — confirming that workload-based governance, which relies on human capacity to assess and signal overload, is unreliable as a safety mechanism.

2. **Measurement difficulty in low-resource settings**: Physiological workload sensors (EEG headsets, eye-tracking glasses) are expensive, require calibration, and are impractical for small-scale fishers in an open boat in rough conditions. Proxy measures (response latency) require continuous human-computer interaction that may not exist in this context.

3. **Conflates human capacity with task safety**: Two scenarios — (a) calm weather, complex task, high workload; (b) storm conditions, simple task, low workload — require different governance. A workload-based trigger would provide AI assistance in (b) but restrict it in (a), which is the reverse of the safety-optimal response. McGrath et al. (2025, CHAI-T) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) note that "when optimal levels of trust are exceeded... the resulting overreliance and lack of monitoring can have catastrophic outcomes" — a workload trigger that provides more AI assistance precisely when conditions are most dangerous maximises this risk.

4. **Not generalizable across users**: Human workload varies by individual experience, training, and physiological state. A workload threshold valid for one fisher may be unsafe for another. Bach et al. (2024) identify user characteristics — including experience, education, and personality traits — as a primary moderating factor in trust calibration; a workload trigger that ignores these individual differences cannot provide consistent governance across users.

### 2.5 Cost Function

**What it is**: An optimization objective function from decision theory or optimal control, encoding the relative costs of different outcomes (e.g., cost of false positive vs. false negative AI recommendation).

**Why it fails as a governance trigger**:

1. **Cost functions are optimization targets, not safety constraints**: They express preferences between outcomes but do not enforce that unsafe outcomes are impossible. A cost function can be satisfied while still allowing unsafe recommendations if the cost assigned to the unsafe outcome is finite. Bajcsy & Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) distinguish precisely between "values" (optimisation objectives) and "needs" (hard constraints that must always be satisfied): "Human values correspond to the optimization objective, whereas human needs correspond to the hard constraints that must always be satisfied." A cost function belongs to the first category; the Safety Dominance Property requires the second.

2. **Reward hacking and misspecification**: Cost functions are notoriously difficult to specify correctly. A cost function that governs AI participation can be gamed — the AI may find policies that minimize the cost function while violating the intent of the governance. Environmental state classification (SAFE/CAUTION/UNSAFE) does not have this property — the classification is based on physical observables, not on AI-optimizable quantities. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) note that the Guaranteed Safe AI framework specifically separates the world model and safety specification from the AI policy precisely to prevent this circularity.

3. **No interpretable governance modes**: A cost function produces a continuous output, not discrete governance modes. The SAFE/CAUTION/UNSAFE distinction is precisely what makes governance interpretable to humans and formally tractable (containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ ∅ requires discrete modes). Schrills et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Questioning%20Trust%20in%20AI%20Research-%20Exploring%20the%20Influence%20of%20Trust%20Assessment%20on%20Dependence%20in%20AI-Assisted%20Decision-Making.md) find that communicated system state (analogous to governance mode) shapes user reliance behaviour more directly than subjective trust attitudes — continuous cost function outputs cannot serve this communication function.

### 2.6 Reward Function

**What it is**: An RL reward signal encoding feedback about AI action quality, used to shape governance via reinforcement.

**Why it fails as a governance trigger**:

1. **Requires operational data to learn**: Reward-based governance requires the system to operate and receive feedback in order to adapt governance. In safety-critical systems, this means the system must first make potentially unsafe decisions to learn which governance level is appropriate — unacceptable in maritime contexts. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) document that shields, by contrast, are computed entirely from a formal specification before deployment, providing guarantees during both training and deployment.

2. **Reward hacking is a known failure mode**: An AI system governing its own participation via reward maximization will exploit the reward signal. This is particularly dangerous when the reward signal is not perfectly aligned with real-world safety. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) identify reward hacking as a primary malfunction risk in frontier AI systems, noting that AI systems may find policies that "satisfy the reward signal while violating the intent of the governance."

3. **Not stable at deployment time**: Reward-based governance evolves with experience. The governance behaviour at deployment may differ substantially from governance behaviour after extended operation — making formal safety proofs that apply at deployment time potentially invalid at run time. Bloomfield & Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) emphasise that assurably guarded systems require guards that can be assured conventionally and remain stable; reward-driven governance cannot satisfy this requirement.

4. **No formal safety property**: The Safety Dominance Property requires that AI recommendations are always bounded by A_AI(S). A reward-driven governance cannot provide this guarantee — it provides expected-case behaviour, not worst-case safety bounds. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) explicitly argue that "empirical evaluations are fundamentally inadequate for safety-critical AI" and that formal verifiable guarantees are required.

### 2.7 Performance Degradation

**What it is**: A measure of how much the AI model's output quality has declined from a baseline — the trigger used by Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) in the traffic-light degradation indexing framework.

**Why it fails as a governance trigger** (and why Flehmig is the primary comparator rather than the proposed solution):

1. **Reactive, not proactive**: Performance degradation is detected *after* the model has already been exposed to conditions it cannot handle well. Environmental state governance is triggered *before* AI recommendations are generated under unsafe conditions — it is proactive. In maritime safety, a reactive trigger may respond too late. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) identify this distinction as central to the difference between design-time constraints (analogous to environmental state classification) and runtime monitoring-based interventions: the latter "requires the AI to already be generating outputs in degraded conditions" before governance can respond.

2. **Circular dependency**: Assessing performance degradation requires monitoring the AI model's outputs — which requires the AI to be actively generating outputs. If the AI is degraded, the monitoring system must still function correctly to detect it. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) address this by specifying that "the goal of the implemented framework is to leave out AI technologies and fully rely on non-AI technologies to monitor the AI component" — but this principle applies equally to the governance trigger itself, which my architecture satisfies through the deterministic environmental classification function f(E).

3. **Conflates model health with environmental danger**: A model can degrade for reasons unrelated to environmental safety — concept drift from seasonal data changes, hardware issues, software updates. Conversely, the environment can become dangerous while the model performs well on the available input (because the dangerous condition is not yet reflected in the model's input features). Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) acknowledge concept drift, temporal degradation, and catastrophic forgetting as distinct operational challenges — none of which are equivalent to the environmental state becoming dangerous.

4. **No advisory scope governance**: The critical finding from the Flehmig analysis is that the traffic-light framework governs **supervisory monitoring intensity**, not **AI advisory scope**. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) state explicitly: at Level 2 (orange), "AI stays as safety function" and "supervisory component must conduct thorough checks, investigate root cause" — but the AI continues to recommend with the same scope as at Level 1. The CAUTION mode in the proposed architecture does something qualitatively different: it restricts what the AI is allowed to recommend, not merely how closely it is monitored. This distinction — which Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) identify as a gap they acknowledge — is the architectural contribution.

5. **Trigger is AI performance, not environment**: Flehmig's governance is triggered when AI performance degrades below a threshold. The proposed architecture is triggered when environmental conditions classify as CAUTION or UNSAFE — independently of AI performance. The former governs the AI; the latter governs the situation. As Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) note, their framework "novelty" lies in indexing AI degradation — not in classifying the situation — confirming these are distinct governance logics.

## 3. Why Environmental Safety State Is the Correct Trigger

### 3.1 Causal Primacy

Environmental conditions are **causally upstream** of all other governance signals:

```
Environment (E)
     ├── directly causes → Real-world danger
     ├── affects → AI input quality → AI confidence / uncertainty
     ├── affects → Human cognitive load → Human workload
     └── over time → Model performance degradation
```

Governing on E addresses the root cause. Governing on AI confidence, uncertainty, or performance addresses downstream symptoms of the same cause. Environmental state governance is more fundamental. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) document this causal structure empirically: in coastal Indonesian fisheries, "wind velocity, wave height, precipitation, season, and vessel size" are the primary inputs to fishers' go/no-go decisions, with all other factors — including personal capacity and economic need — treated as downstream considerations. Bajcsy & Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) formalise the same logic in their Human-AI safety framework, establishing that "present actions which do not appear to violate constraints can still steer the system into states from which it is impossible to avoid catastrophic failures in the future" — a finding that demands governance be triggered by upstream environmental state, not by downstream evidence of problems already emerging.

### 3.2 Independent Observability

Environmental state can be measured by **sensors independent of the AI system** — anemometers, wave buoys, weather stations, GPS, visual inspection. This means:

- Governance functions even if the AI model is degraded or unavailable
- Governance can be verified by the human operator from direct physical observation
- There is no circular dependency between the governed system and the governance trigger

This is a critical safety property. Any governance trigger that depends on AI outputs (confidence, uncertainty, performance) creates a potential single point of failure: if the AI fails, both the recommendation and the governance assessment may be compromised simultaneously. Bloomfield & Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) identify this as the distinction between *assuredly guarded* systems (guards use no AI/ML) and *unassuredly guarded* systems (guards depend on AI/ML): "assuredly guarded systems" are those whose guards "do not themselves use AI/ML and can therefore be assured conventionally." Environmental state classification from physical sensors constitutes exactly this kind of assurably guarded governance. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) specify the same requirement in their Guaranteed Safe AI framework: the world model providing the safety specification must be independent of the AI policy, so that the verifier's guarantee is not circular. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) confirm the same principle in the shielding literature: "a shield maintains a clear separation between an agent's safety and performance," and the shield is "computed from a formal specification + an abstract model of the environment, independent of the agent's learning objective."

### 3.3 Human Interpretability

For a human-in-the-loop decision support system, governance mode changes must be **understandable to the human operator**:

| Trigger type | Example display | Interpretable to fisher? |
|---|---|---|
| Environmental state | "CAUTION: Wind 22 knots, Waves 2.1m" | **Yes** — directly observable |
| AI confidence | "Confidence: 0.61" | No — abstract score |
| Model uncertainty | "Epistemic uncertainty: 0.24" | No — technical concept |
| Risk score | "Risk index: 67/100" | Partially — but opaque to audit |
| Performance degradation | "Model accuracy: 78% (↓12%)" | No — requires training baseline |

Trust calibration requires that users understand *why* governance mode changed (McGrath et al., 2025, CHAI-T [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md); Bach et al., 2024). McGrath et al. (2025, CHAI-T) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) state that "trust in a machine system is considered to have reached its optimal level... when the human trustor's expectations are aligned with the capabilities of system" — a condition that can only be met when governance rationale is legible. Environmental state provides the clearest governance rationale. Atf & Lewis (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Is%20Trust%20Correlated%20With%20Explainability%20in%20AI%3F%20A%20Meta-Analysis.md) distinguish between "trust enforcement" (presenting selective data to instil trust) and "trust empowerment" (providing all relevant information enabling users to calibrate their own trust level); environmental state disclosure is an archetypal trust empowerment mechanism — the fisher can independently verify "waves 2.1m" against direct perception and form their own assessment. Schrills et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Questioning%20Trust%20in%20AI%20Research-%20Exploring%20the%20Influence%20of%20Trust%20Assessment%20on%20Dependence%20in%20AI-Assisted%20Decision-Making.md) find empirically that "instructed reliability appears to be more influential in determining behaviour compared to assessed trust" — which implies that governance state communication grounded in interpretable physical parameters produces more reliable behavioural calibration than governance based on opaque technical scores.

### 3.4 Formal Tractability

The Safety Dominance Property requires:

> **AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S)** for all E, where S = f(E)

For this to be a provable safety property, S must be a **deterministic function of observable quantities**. Environmental state satisfies this: f(E) is a deterministic classification function over measurable variables. The formal proof proceeds by case analysis over S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE}.

If S were a function of AI confidence, model uncertainty, or a reward signal, the proof would be conditional on those estimates being correct — which cannot be guaranteed. If S were a function of a learned risk score, the proof would be conditional on the risk model being correctly specified — which cannot be formally verified without circularity. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) define formally that a Guaranteed Safe AI system requires a verifier Vψ that "produces an auditable proof certificate that the AI satisfies the specification relative to the world model" — a certificate that is only achievable when the world model and specification are independent of the AI policy. Bajcsy & Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) prove Theorem 1 (their safety filter guarantee) under the analogous requirement that the safety monitor Δ be computed from a world model independent of the task policy. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) demonstrate formally that shields provide either absolute safety guarantees (never visit unsafe states) or probabilistic bounds (P(unsafe) ≤ ε) — guarantees contingent on the environment model, not on AI self-assessment. Environmental state governance satisfies all three frameworks' independence requirements; AI-based governance triggers do not.

### 3.5 Domain Empirical Validation

Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) documents that Penang fishers already make tripartite decisions based on environmental conditions:
- **Go** — conditions acceptable
- **Cautious-go** — conditions elevated but manageable (reduced activity, shortened trip)
- **Don't-go** — conditions unsafe (stay ashore)

Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) reports environmental factor importance ratings from 25 semi-structured interviews: tide (4.55/5), fishing resource (4.45/5), previous catch (4.38/5), and weather (3.75/5) are the dominant inputs to daily operational decisions, with safety concern rated 3.40/5. The study documents explicit fisher statements such as "If the wind is too strong, I don't go" — confirming that rule-based, environmental-state-conditioned participation governance is already embedded in fisher practice. This is not an abstraction imposed on the domain. Fishers already use environmental state to classify operational modes and adjust decision behaviour accordingly. The proposed architecture **formalises existing practice** — it provides formal governance for a decision structure that domain practitioners have evolved empirically.

Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) corroborate this pattern in an Indonesian coastal fisheries context: they document that during adverse weather (the West season, November–February, with winds of 30–40 knots and waves exceeding 2m), fishers adaptively "shift from open-sea to near-shore operations" and reduce trip frequency from 5–6 to 2–3 per week and duration from 7–10 hours to 3–5 hours — constituting a naturally occurring graduated, state-conditioned restriction of operational scope with no formal decision support system to organise it. The study also finds that the binary governmental warning from Indonesia's Ministry of Maritime Affairs ("don't sail") is routinely ignored under economic pressure, demonstrating that binary governance is inadequate in this domain and that a graduated model aligning with fishers' existing adaptive behaviour is required.

The most recent primary empirical study of the exact target population — Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md), surveying 136 small-scale fishers in central Terengganu — provides the strongest quantitative validation that the E vector parameters reflect the population's own risk perception hierarchy. Fisher-reported climate hazards show near-unanimous agreement on the same environmental components: stronger monsoonal winds and larger waves (95%), more intense weather events (91%), and erratic rainfall patterns (91%) map directly onto the dynamic components w (wind), o (ocean/wave), and r (rainfall). The study's empirical knowledge item — "I know if it's suddenly windy/change of weather, I can't go fishing" — is direct primary evidence that wind condition (w) is a binary go/no-go trigger in current practice. Critically, Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) document that traditional weather prediction ability is declining among this population as climate unpredictability intensifies, creating a *decision support vacuum* at intermediate risk states that the proposed architecture is designed to fill: fishers are increasingly reliant on weather apps (Windy, Windfinder) for raw data but have no system that translates environmental conditions into scope-restricted decision support. Furthermore, the study identifies flexibility as the *weakest* adaptive capacity domain — only 58% of fishers are willing to consider alternative livelihoods and the binary go/no-go pattern is documented as the sole decision mode. This reframes the CAUTION mode's significance: it does not merely restrict AI advisory scope, it provides the graduated decision support infrastructure that enables a type of adaptive response the population cannot currently execute without AI assistance.

Earlier contextual work — Shaffril et al. (2017) [[notes]](../notes/Adapting%20towards%20climate%20change%20impacts-%20Strategies%20for%20small-scale%20fishermen%20in%20Malaysia%20.md) — establishes historical background for the domain: Malaysian small-scale fishermen operating vessels ≤22 feet within <5 nautical miles of shore have historically made go/no-go decisions based on observable environmental conditions (wind, rainfall, marine warnings, ocean state) under resource constraints (low income, MCE-or-lower education, ages >40). This historical characterisation is retained as background context. Where current population data is needed, Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md) — published in a Q1 journal based on 2023 primary fieldwork — is the appropriate primary citation.

Ryu & Han (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Environment-Aware%20Multi-Sensor%20Fusion%20for%20Maritime%20Domain%20Awareness-%20A%20Comprehensive%20Review.md), in a comprehensive review of environment-aware multi-sensor fusion for maritime domain awareness (*Korean Journal of Remote Sensing*, Vol. 41, No. 6), provide the strongest domain evidence from the sensing and detection literature for why environmental state must be the governance trigger. Their review demonstrates that environmental conditions — wind speed, wave height, swell period, and atmospheric state — are first-order determinants of detection reliability across *all* maritime sensing modalities: SAR (wind-speed-dependent backscatter), optical (visibility, illumination, glare), AIS (propagation attenuation), and RF (atmospheric ducting, clutter). Critically, all modalities degrade simultaneously under adverse environmental conditions, meaning that no sensor can compensate for another when the environment deteriorates. Ryu & Han (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Environment-Aware%20Multi-Sensor%20Fusion%20for%20Maritime%20Domain%20Awareness-%20A%20Comprehensive%20Review.md) identify three wind-speed regimes with distinct detection behaviours: below ~2 m/s (SAFE-analogue — calm conditions, some sensors underperform), 2–8 m/s (CAUTION-analogue — optimal for most sensors but approaching degradation), and above ~10–12 m/s (UNSAFE-analogue — white-cap contamination, breaking waves, sea clutter overwhelms detection algorithms). These empirically documented regimes map directly onto the proposed S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE} classification and provide independent physical evidence for the E vector components w (wind) and o (ocean state). The review further documents that fixed detection thresholds fail under non-stationary environmental clutter — confirming that static governance configurations are inadequate for maritime environments and that governance must be conditioned on dynamically classified environmental state. DL-based detection models trained under calm conditions exhibit domain shift when deployed in adverse weather, reinforcing the argument that environmental state is causally upstream of AI reliability and must be the governance trigger rather than AI-internal signals.

No such empirical validation exists for AI confidence-based, uncertainty-based, or performance-based governance in fisheries or maritime decision-making.

### 3.6 Proactive Safety

Environmental state governance is **predictive**: conditions classify as CAUTION before an accident occurs, enabling the AI to reduce its advisory scope before it faces conditions it cannot handle. The governance is triggered by the situation, not by evidence of AI failure.

This is architecturally analogous to the Operational Design Domain (ODD) confinement approach in automated driving (Gyllenhammar et al., 2025 [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md)) — the system knows its operational boundaries and governs itself accordingly. Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) document the MRC/ROD hierarchy (Minimal Risk Condition and Restricted Operational Domain) as an approach to graduated operational restriction in automotive safety — "ODD → ROD → MRC" — recognising that graduated restriction is preferable to binary blocking. The key difference in the proposed architecture is that ODD and ROD are design-time constraints, while environmental state governance is a runtime classification applied continuously. Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), reviewing 91 papers on collaborative intelligence for safety-critical industries, confirm that no reviewed paper implements proactive, state-conditioned restriction of AI advisory scope — the gap that environmental state governance fills. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) further document that all 11 major Frontier AI Safety Frameworks implement participation governance (Level 1) triggered by capability assessments, but none implement state-conditioned advisory scope restriction (Level 2) — confirming that proactive, graduated governance based on operational environment remains an open problem across the field.

## 5. The Remaining Question: What About Combining Triggers?

A reasonable objection is: why not combine environmental state with AI confidence or uncertainty for a more comprehensive governance trigger?

The response is threefold:

1. **Formal safety requires a single authoritative classification**: The containment property A_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ ∅ and the Safety Dominance Property require S to be a well-defined, deterministic classification. A composite trigger (e.g., SAFE if environment SAFE AND confidence > 0.8) creates ambiguous states and complicates formal proofs. Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) specify that a safety specification must be a "mathematical description of what effects are acceptable" — a specification that is partially dependent on AI self-assessment violates the independence requirement for formal verification. Bajcsy & Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) prove their safety guarantee (Theorem 1) under the assumption that the safety monitor is independent of the task policy; a composite trigger incorporating AI confidence would violate this assumption.

2. **Additive AI-side triggers are a separate concern**: AI confidence, uncertainty, and performance degradation are appropriate triggers for *internal AI monitoring* (as Flehmig et al., 2024 [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md), propose through their non-AI monitoring component and supervisory layer). They can coexist as monitoring signals without being governance triggers. The proposed architecture can incorporate an internal AI monitor that raises alerts; the governance mode is determined by environment alone. Flehmig et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) themselves state the principle: "the goal of the implemented framework is to leave out AI technologies and fully rely on non-AI technologies to monitor the AI component" — which is the correct function for AI-side signals.

3. **Parsimony for low-resource deployment**: In resource-constrained edge deployments, every additional governance component adds computational overhead and potential failure points. Environmental state classification from a small set of sensor readings is computationally lightweight, robust, and requires no model inference — making it appropriate for intermittent-connectivity, low-power deployment. Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) note the SWaP (Size, Weight, Power) constraints on edge-deployed safety-critical AI systems and observe that lightweight, reliable safety mechanisms are preferable to complex hybrid triggers. Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) similarly note performance degradation in resource-constrained and low-connectivity deployment contexts; a governance trigger requiring model inference compounds these constraints.

## 6. One-Paragraph Summary (for use in Chapter 2 or 3)

> Environmental safety state is selected as the governance trigger because it is causally upstream of all other candidate signals — environmental conditions *cause* both real-world danger and degraded AI performance; AI confidence, uncertainty, and performance are downstream symptoms. This causal primacy is confirmed empirically across multiple independent fisheries studies. Yamin et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Interplay%20of%20traditional%20knowledge%20and%20adaptive%20capacity%20in%20climate%20change%20adaptation%20of%20small-scale%20fishers%20in%20central%20Terengganu%2C%20Malaysia%20.md), in a primary survey of 136 small-scale fishers in central Terengganu (the exact target population), find near-unanimous fisher-reported identification of wind/waves (95%), weather intensity (91%), and erratic rainfall (91%) as the primary climate hazards — mapping directly onto E vector components w, o, and r; the empirical knowledge item "if it's suddenly windy/change of weather, I can't go fishing" confirms that environmental state is already the binary go/no-go trigger in current practice. Gao (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Mapping%20the%20decision-making%20factors%20of%20small-scale%20fishers-%20a%20case%20study%20of%20Penang.md) records that Penang fishers make tripartite (go / cautious-go / don't-go) decisions from environmental state. Rahim et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Survival%20Decisions%20and%20Adaptation%20Strategies%20of%20Small-scale%20Fishers%20in%20the%20Face%20of%20Extreme%20Weather%20Impacts%20in%20Coastal%20Areas.md) document that wind velocity, wave height, and seasonal conditions are the primary inputs to coastal fisher go/no-go decisions in Indonesia. The proposed architecture formalises existing domain practice rather than imposing an external abstraction. Environmental state is independently observable via non-AI sensors, making governance robust to AI system failure and free from circular dependency; Bloomfield & Rushby (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Assurance%20of%20AI%20Systems%20From%20a%20Dependability%20Perspective.md) identify this independence as the defining property of *assuredly guarded* systems, and Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) confirm it in their shielding framework: "a shield maintains a clear separation between an agent's safety and performance." Environmental state is human-interpretable — Atf & Lewis (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Is%20Trust%20Correlated%20With%20Explainability%20in%20AI%3F%20A%20Meta-Analysis.md) establish through meta-analysis of 90 studies (r = 0.194) that explainability has only a weak effect on trust, making the case that governance rationale must be grounded in independently verifiable physical parameters rather than technical scores; McGrath et al. (2025, CHAI-T) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Human-AI%20Trust%20(CHAI-T)-%20A%20Process%20Framework%20for%20Active%20Management%20of%20Trust%20in%20Human-AI%20Collaboration.md) establish that optimal trust requires expectation-capability alignment, which environmental state governance enables by communicating legible, verifiable conditions. It is formally tractable: the Safety Dominance Property AI(E) ⊆ A_AI(S) is provable when S = f(E) is a deterministic function of observables, satisfying the independence requirement specified by Dalrymple et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) and Bajcsy & Fisac (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) for formal safety guarantees. It is proactive: governance mode changes in response to environmental conditions before AI recommendations are generated under dangerous inputs, analogous to the ODD confinement approach documented by Gyllenhammar et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) in automated driving. And it addresses a gap confirmed across all major safety-critical AI domains: Ramos et al. (2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) find no implementation of state-conditioned advisory scope restriction across 91 collaborative intelligence papers, and Bengio et al. (2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) confirm that all 11 major Frontier AI Safety Frameworks implement only Level 1 (participation) governance, not Level 2 (advisory scope) governance conditioned on operational environment — the precisely defined gap that environmental safety state governance fills.
