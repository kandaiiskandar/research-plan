# Is This Really New? The Case for Novelty

**Document type**: Gap argument / examiner response
**For**: Chapter 2 — Literature Summary and Research Gap
**Question**: Has anyone already proposed a state-based governance architecture that simultaneously controls AI participation and advisory scope?

---

## 1. How to Read the Gap Argument

The examiner's challenge is reasonable. AI governance has attracted substantial research attention, and the literature contains sophisticated safety shields, formal verification frameworks, guardrail taxonomies, adaptive autonomy systems, and international policy frameworks. The answer to the examiner's question is not that governance has been ignored. It is that every existing mechanism implements governance at only one of two necessary levels — and no existing work unifies both levels under the same classified environmental state variable.

The proposed architecture implements a two-level governance pair (G(S), A\_AI(S)) conditioned on a classified environmental safety state S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE}, where S = f(E) and E is a vector of observable environmental parameters. G(S) determines whether AI may participate at all; A\_AI(S) determines what AI is permitted to recommend; the Safety Dominance Property guarantees AI(E) ⊆ A\_AI(S) for all E; and the containment relationship A\_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A\_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A\_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅ ensures graduated restriction as conditions deteriorate.

The gap is not the absence of participation control — that is well-established. The gap is not the absence of output filtering — that too is well-studied. The gap is the absence of a unified mechanism where the same classified environmental state simultaneously and formally governs both levels. The argument proceeds category by category through the specific evidence from the notes, confirmed by four independent systematic reviews covering 91 + 46 + 11 + survey bodies of literature.

## 2. Safety Shields and Formal Participation Control — Level 1 Only

The most formally rigorous AI governance mechanisms in the literature are safety shields and safety filter architectures. They implement Level 1 governance — controlling whether AI acts at all — with mathematical guarantees. None of them implements Level 2 advisory scope governance conditioned on classified environmental state.

**Könighofer et al. (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) present safety shields for reinforcement learning as the archetypal runtime AI governance mechanism. A shield is computed from a formal safety specification plus an abstract environment model and operates alongside the RL agent: in pre-shielding mode, the shield provides the set of safe actions before the agent selects; in post-shielding mode, the shield intercepts and replaces unsafe actions after selection. The shield classifies states into safe states (the winning region, from which the agent can always avoid unsafe states) and dangerous or unsafe states (outside the winning region, where fallback is triggered). This is a binary participation gate: inside the winning region, AI operates; outside it, fallback takes over.

Within the winning region, the shield's safe action set varies per state — different states permit different actions. This is the closest existing mechanism to A\_AI(S) in the entire shield literature. But the notes are explicit on where this falls short: "the shield classifies actions as safe/unsafe per individual state-action pair, not by classifying the environmental state into discrete governance modes (SAFE/CAUTION/UNSAFE) with different advisory scope types." The probabilistic threshold ε produces shields of different restrictiveness, and Könighofer et al. [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) empirically demonstrate that intermediate-restrictiveness shields (ε = 0.03, 0.05) outperform both no restriction and maximum restriction — a direct empirical motivation for a CAUTION mode. But ε is a design-time parameter, not a runtime state-conditioned mode switch. There is no CAUTION equivalent: "the shield has no intermediate mode where the agent is permitted but with restricted scope."

**Bajcsy & Fisac (2024)** [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) present the Human-AI Safety Filter — formally the closest to the proposed governance pair of any paper in the review. Their filter is a tuple (π^AI\_fallback, Δ, ϕ): a fallback policy, a safety monitor, and an intervention scheme. The monitor evaluates whether the task policy's proposed action could lead to future safety violations; the intervention scheme either permits or replaces the action. Theorem 1 guarantees that the system never enters the failure set for all allowable human behaviours — the strongest formal safety guarantee in the review. The filter is state-conditioned: the set of permissible AI actions depends on the current state z^AI via the safety value function V(z^AI).

Yet the governance is binary: "the intervention scheme is a simple binary switch (at each time, apply task policy if deemed safe, else apply fallback)." There is no intermediate state where AI participates under restricted recommendation scope. The filter permits or overrides — there is no CAUTION mode. The notes characterise this precisely: "even this state-of-the-art, formally guaranteed framework implements binary (permit/override) governance, not graduated governance with an intermediate restricted-participation mode."

**Dalrymple et al. (2024)** [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) establish the Guaranteed Safe AI framework, defining a GS AI system formally as a tuple ⟨π, m, ψ, V_ψ⟩ where the verifier V_ψ produces a proof certificate that the policy π satisfies the safety specification ψ relative to the world model m. The verifier gates whether AI output reaches the world. This implements Level 1 governance at the policy level — the policy either passes verification or is disabled and backed up. The notes confirm: "there is no environmental state classification. The governance is policy-level (verify the whole policy) not state-level (restrict recommendations per classified state)." The safety specification constrains what AI may do globally but does not vary this constraint across classified environmental states. No equivalent of A\_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A\_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A\_AI(UNSAFE) exists anywhere in the framework.

**Wang et al. (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/AgentSpec-%20Customizable%20Runtime%20Enforcement%20for%20Safe%20and%20Reliable%20LLM%20Agents.md) contribute AgentSpec — described as "the first framework that systematically enforces customizable safety constraints on LLM agents at runtime." AgentSpec defines rules as three-tuples r = (η\_r, P\_r, E\_r): triggering events, predicates, and enforcement mechanisms (stop, user\_inspection, invoke\_action, llm\_self\_examine). The framework achieves >90% unsafe code execution prevention, 100% compliance in AV law-violation scenarios, and millisecond-level overhead. Its architecture formally decouples safety enforcement from LLM reasoning. But the governance is per-action: each planned action is evaluated against individual rules, and the agent's action space A is the same regardless of environmental conditions. The notes identify the critical gap: "AgentSpec does not define a state-conditioned admissible action space. It evaluates individual actions against individual rules. There is no formal structure that says 'under safety state S, only recommendation types {R₁, R₂} are permitted.'" There is no S = f(E) layer classifying the environment, and no CAUTION mode where G(S) = 1 but A\_AI is restricted.

**Abella et al. (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/SAFEXPLAIN-%20a%20Complete%20Approach%20Towards%20Trustworthy%20AI-Based%20Safety-Critical%20Systems.md) present SAFEXPLAIN — the most comprehensive cross-domain AI safety architecture for physical systems, spanning automotive, space, and railway with a 3-year EU Horizon Europe mandate. Its reference safety architecture includes diverse AI redundancy, a supervision function providing a safe envelope, hierarchical diagnostics (L1/L2/L3), a non-AI fallback subsystem, and a safety control function. The supervision function bounds AI operations within predefined safety limits — the closest existing concept to A\_AI(S) in the engineering domain. But the notes are clear: "runtime governance remains binary: the safety control either accepts AI output or switches to fallback/safe state. The supervision function's safe envelope constrains AI output scope, but this constraint is statically configured per DL usage level at design time, not dynamically conditioned on classified environmental state at runtime." The DL usage levels that configure different output types (suggestion vs. decision) are design-time categories, not runtime state-conditioned governance.

The gap is further confirmed by the formal methods literature. **Newcomb & Ochoa (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) systematically review 46 peer-reviewed studies on formal methods for safety-critical machine learning across eight method categories: reachability and over-approximation, SMT-based verification, MILP/ILP, model checking, runtime verification, shielding, control barrier functions, and risk verification. Their conclusion is unambiguous: every method surveyed operates with binary safety boundaries — safe/unsafe, permit/block, property-satisfied/violated. No paper classifies states into three levels with differentiated AI behaviour across levels. No paper implements state-conditioned advisory scope restriction. No paper defines a Safety Dominance Property for graduated governance. Newcomb & Ochoa (2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) extend the binary governance gap claim from applied architecture into the most formally rigorous corner of the ML safety literature.

## 3. Adaptive Autonomy and Graduated Systems — Mode Changes Without Scope Restriction

A second category of literature implements graduated operational modes. These papers recognise that a binary on/off switch is too coarse, and introduce intermediate levels. The gap is that the intermediate level governs supervisory behaviour or operational domain restriction — never AI advisory scope.

**Flehmig et al. (2024)** [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) present the closest structural precedent to the proposed tripartite safety state model. Their traffic-light degradation index classifies AI operational status into three levels: Level 1 (green) — no significant degradation, AI stays as safety function; Level 2 (orange) — signs of degradation, AI stays but supervisory component must conduct thorough checks; Level 3 (red) — degradation surpassed threshold, switch to non-AI backup. The paper authors themselves claim novelty: "To our knowledge, there is currently no existing framework or method for indexing AI degradation in safety-critical systems in such a manner."

The architectural parallels are striking. The framework has a layered structure (AI component + non-AI monitoring + non-AI supervisory component + limiting logic + deterministic backup) that maps onto the hybrid governance architecture. The three-level classification maps structurally onto SAFE/CAUTION/UNSAFE. But the notes identify the decisive difference: "the traffic-light index governs the supervisory response intensity, not the AI's operational scope." At both Level 1 and Level 2, the AI operates with identical, unrestricted advisory scope. Level 2 changes what the human supervisor does — it does not restrict what the AI may recommend. There is no A\_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A\_AI(SAFE). The gap is at the Level 2 governance position.

**Gyllenhammar et al. (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) review safety evidence methods for automated driving systems in a comprehensive IEEE Transactions survey spanning design-time methods, verification, runtime risk assessment, and runtime self-adaptation. The MRC/ROD hierarchy (Fig. 11 of that paper) introduces intermediate operational restriction: ODD → ROD (Restricted Operational Domain) → MRC (Minimal Risk Condition). This is graduated degradation — the system operates at reduced capability in ROD before falling back to the stopped MRC state. The operational space partition into safe, warning, and catastrophic states exists in the monitoring literature reviewed. But the notes confirm: "No reviewed paper classifies environmental conditions into discrete safety states that then determine what types of recommendations AI may produce." The ROD restricts what the system may operationally attempt; it does not restrict what types of recommendations AI generates. The trigger is system degradation, not environmental state classification. The dominant paradigm across the entire automotive domain — the most mature safety-critical AI domain — is binary switching between nominal and safety channels.

**Li et al. (2026)** present PSE-DRL, a safety-enhanced deep reinforcement learning method for autonomous driving with three mechanisms: anticipated risk evaluation, dangerous experience enhancement, and reckless action prevention via dynamic risk constraints. When the anticipated risk function J(s\_t, a\_t) exceeds the threshold J\_thres, the unsafe action is blocked and a safe alternative a\* is generated from the admissible action space. This is per-action binary correction: each action is individually evaluated and corrected if unsafe. The notes confirm: "the AI always generates full-scope actions; only individual unsafe actions are corrected." The dynamic threshold J\_thres decreases over training episodes — it is a training schedule parameter, not a runtime state-conditioned governance mechanism. There is no environmental state classification, no graduated governance modes, and no restriction of action types by state.

**Baxi (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) warrants extended treatment and receives it in Section 10 below, as the structurally closest prior work to the proposed architecture.

## 4. Guardrails and Output Filtering — Level 2 Without Level 1 State Conditioning

The guardrails literature implements what might be called Level 2 governance — constraining what AI may output — but does so statically, without conditioning the output restriction on classified environmental safety state. The restriction is per-output-content, not per-safety-state-type.

**Shamsujjoha et al. (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) contribute the most comprehensive taxonomy of runtime guardrails for AI agents. Their Swiss Cheese Model reference architecture and SLR of 32 studies produce a taxonomy across 13 guardrail action types (block, filter, flag, modify, validate, parallel calls, retry, fall back, human intervention, defer, isolate, redundancy, evaluate), 14 quality attributes, and 14 pipeline targets. This is the definitive catalogue of what governance actions exist. But the taxonomy answers the question "what can be done to AI output?" — not "when should which governance configuration apply based on environmental conditions?" Guardrail activation is per-artifact and per-quality-attribute, not per-classified-environmental-safety-state. The context-dependent rule type, which "adjusts the implementation of guardrails based on the system's specific operational context," is the closest concept to state-conditioned governance in the guardrail literature — but "context" here refers to data or deployment context, not classified environmental safety state. There is no S = f(E) activation mechanism. The taxonomy comprehensively catalogues the governance action space; the proposed architecture provides the state-conditioned selection mechanism that selects from that space.

**Chen et al. (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md) provide a comprehensive survey of the AI safety landscape for LLMs, formally defining the Guardrail System as an AI pipeline with input module → LLM → output module. Their AI Safety Principle I (Output Constraint) states: Y ∩ Z\_i = ∅ — the output space must be disjoint from prohibited output sets. This is the closest formal analogue to the Safety Dominance Property in the survey literature, but it is static and binary: the prohibited set Z does not vary with operational or environmental context. As the notes observe: "In all guardrail implementations reviewed, the outcome is binary: the response is either delivered or blocked/rejected. There is no mechanism where AI still participates but with restricted output types — no analogue to the CAUTION state where A\_AI(CAUTION) ⊂ A\_AI(SAFE)." The OpenChatKit moderation model classifies input into five categories including caution levels, but this classification results in a binary deliver/reject outcome, not graduated advisory scope.

**Liang et al. (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md) instantiate the Dalrymple et al. guaranteed safe AI framework in 6G semantic communication networks, making this a concrete implementation of the safeguarded AI architecture. Their gatekeeper applies one of four filtering techniques (simple threshold, random smoothing, conformal prediction, Monte Carlo dropout) between AI model output and the application operator. The conformal prediction technique provides a formal per-output coverage guarantee: P(true output ∈ predicted set) ≥ 1 − α. The paper documents the empirical cost of binary governance: higher gatekeeper thresholds improve output quality but increase failure rates — at τ = 0.7, highest BLEU scores but also highest failure rates at low SNR. This accuracy-failure trade-off is precisely the problem the CAUTION state resolves by enabling restricted-scope AI output rather than outright rejection. The notes confirm: "when the gatekeeper rejects output, the system fails (no output at all), rather than providing restricted-scope output. The choice is full output or no output." The paper mentions dynamic threshold adjustment based on channel conditions as a possibility but does not formalise or implement it as state-conditioned governance.

## 5. Hybrid AI — Architecture Without Governance

Two hybrid AI papers address how to combine deterministic rules with probabilistic AI for accuracy and reliability in safety-critical domains. Both validate the architectural principle that deterministic components must govern probabilistic AI. Neither addresses the governance question of when AI participates and what it is permitted to recommend.

**Mussi et al. (2025)** provide an interdisciplinary perspective on human-AI interaction in safety-critical network infrastructures across power grids, railway networks, and air traffic management. Their knowledge-assisted AI pattern integrates "human-devised safety constraints into AI models as a foundation of reliability upon which learning-based improvements can build" — directly aligned with the deterministic-gate-first approach. The hierarchical decision-making pattern decomposes high-level goals into constrained low-level actions — conceptually parallel to the two-level governance structure. The paper identifies seven common decision-making characteristics across all three domains — environmental uncertainty, time-constrained decisions, large action spaces, conflicting objectives, and the need for human-AI collaboration — that closely parallel the operational conditions addressed by the proposed architecture.

But Mussi et al.'s governance remains at the design-time architectural and organisational level: function allocation, architectural patterns, ALTAI trustworthiness assessment. The notes are precise: "the governance mechanisms described are design-time architectural choices and organisational function allocation, not runtime state-conditioned governance." There is no formal pipeline. There is no S = f(E). There is no (G(S), A\_AI(S)) pair. The paper identifies the ingredients; the proposed architecture formalises them.

**Castagnone & Nitti (2026)** present the SYNAPSE architecture — a neuro-symbolic hybrid for structural engineering that strictly separates neural and symbolic components. The core design principle is that "the neural component is never allowed to perform safety-critical calculations directly." A four-stage verification protocol (syntactic, semantic, dimensional, regulatory compliance) gates LLM-generated scripts before execution on the deterministic engine. The symbolic verification layer prevented any incorrect structural calculations from reaching users across 200+ evaluated queries. The architecture achieves 94% accuracy with a 4.7/5 user satisfaction rating.

SYNAPSE validates the neuro-symbolic approach for safety-critical domains and demonstrates that architectural separation between neural and symbolic components can achieve deterministic reliability. But its governance is static: the LLM's role is permanently fixed as interpretation and presentation, never calculation, regardless of context. The notes explain the structural distinction: "SYNAPSE implements static architectural governance (the LLM's role is permanently fixed) while the proposed architecture implements dynamic state-conditioned governance (AI scope varies by classified environmental safety state)." SYNAPSE does not need graduated governance because the operational task does not change. In contexts where environmental conditions change — where weather deteriorates, where conditions shift — AI advisory scope must contract accordingly. SYNAPSE has no mechanism for this.

These papers confirm that hybrid AI enabling architectures exist for safety-critical domains. They are not governance solutions.

## 6. Formal Methods — Binary Safety Boundaries

The formal verification literature provides the mathematical machinery used to prove properties of AI systems in safety-critical contexts. Its finding, across 46 peer-reviewed studies, is unambiguous: all formal methods operate with binary safety boundaries.

**Newcomb & Ochoa (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) systematically review formal methods for safety-critical machine learning. Across eight categories, the pattern is uniform:

- Shielding: a shield checks each proposed action and corrects if unsafe — binary per action
- Control Barrier Functions: states above the CBF threshold are safe; states below are unsafe — binary threshold
- Reachability analysis: outputs within specified bounds are safe; outside is unsafe — binary containment
- Runtime verification: the property is either satisfied or violated — binary per trace
- Model checking: the specification is either satisfied or violated — binary

"The entire SLR surveys 46 studies across eight formal method categories, and every method operates with binary safety boundaries. No study implements graduated safety states." No surveyed paper restricts AI advisory scope based on classified environmental state. No paper defines a Safety Dominance Property for non-binary governance. The binary governance paradigm is confirmed not merely as a design convention but as a universal pattern across the most formally rigorous approaches to ML safety.

**Vermaelen & Holvoet (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md) present Tumato 2.0, a constraint-based planning approach for autonomous robot behaviour that achieves soundness by construction: the generated policy guarantees ∀(a,s) ∈ policy : allowed(a,s), ensuring no selected action can violate state rules under any foreseeable outcome. The `allowed(a,s)` predicate — an action is allowed only if every possible outcome satisfies all state rules — is formally equivalent to a per-action participation gate. But it is binary: "the system does not classify states into discrete safety levels with different admissible action sets." The authors themselves identify weighted safety constraints as future work, acknowledging that safety constraints are currently "treated with equal weight" and that severity differentiation would be desirable. This is indirect evidence that the binary treatment is a known limitation, not a design choice.

Formal methods provide the mathematical machinery that can be used to prove properties of the proposed governance architecture. They do not implement graduated governance.

## 7. Governance Frameworks — Policy Level, Not Runtime Architecture

The governance literature operates at the policy and regulatory level. It determines which systems are subject to oversight and what compliance obligations they must meet. It does not implement runtime state-conditioned governance of AI advisory scope.

**Bengio et al. (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) — the International AI Safety Report, the only internationally mandated scientific assessment of AI safety with contributors from 30+ countries — map the governance architectures of 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks. The classification schemes are elaborate: Anthropic's ASL-1 through ASL-4+; OpenAI's High/Critical capability levels; Google DeepMind's Critical Capability Levels with alert thresholds; Meta's Moderate/High/Critical tiers; NVIDIA's MR1–MR5 risk scores; Cohere's Low/Medium/High/Very High levels.

These are detailed graduated schemes. But the notes confirm the decisive structural difference: every framework classifies based on model capability — what the AI can do — not environmental state — what conditions the AI operates in. When frameworks escalate risk levels, they apply enhanced security controls, access restrictions, additional monitoring, and deployment restrictions or halts. "They do not define a restricted-but-non-empty recommendation space where AI still participates under constrained advisory scope." The report also notes explicitly: "unlike risk management approaches from other sectors, such as aviation or nuclear power, Frontier AI Safety Frameworks typically do not use explicit quantitative risk thresholds." No framework defines a property comparable to the Safety Dominance Property. No framework implements a CAUTION mode. Level 1 governance is well-established; Level 2 does not exist as a formal governance mechanism in any of the 11 reviewed frameworks.

**Bello y Villarino et al. (2025)** test AI regulatory frameworks against 163 real-world NSW government automated decision-making systems. The EU AI Act applies a binary scope test: AI yes/no, then high-risk yes/no. The NSW AIAF applies a graduated risk scale (low, mid-range, high, very high) with differentiated obligations at each level — mid-range requires piloting, high requires AI Review Committee referral. This is graduated governance at the regulatory level. But the notes confirm: "neither framework constrains the type of AI output based on risk level. Obligations are procedural (assessment, documentation, monitoring, committee review) and apply uniformly to all in-scope systems." The graduated risk scale produces procedural differentiation, not operational constraint graduation. It determines what is required of the deploying agency, not what the AI system may do at runtime.

The finding that rule-based systems in sensitive domains fall outside AI-focused governance (approximately 60% of elevated-risk NSW systems do not qualify as AI under the EU AI Act definition) also supports the case for hybrid architectures that formally integrate deterministic safety governance with AI reasoning, rather than treating them as separate regulatory categories.

**Attard-Frost & Lyons (2025)** [[notes]](../notes/AI%20governance%20systems-%20A%20multi-scale%20analysis%20framework%2C%20empirical%20findings%2C%20and%20future%20directions.%20AI%20and%20Ethics.md) provide the most granular empirical evidence available at the institutional governance layer. Through 20 semi-structured interviews with government leaders and subject matter experts, they map Canada's national AI governance system across 12 analytical dimensions, producing a dataset of 610 governance-related topics — the most comprehensive empirical decomposition of a national AI governance system in the literature. The mapping covers 120 unique actors, 142 unique resources (laws, regulations, guidance documents, auditing frameworks), 40 unique networks, and 21 rule-based constraints. Not one of the 610 topics references runtime environmental state classification, state-conditioned advisory scope restriction, graduated operational modes, or anything analogous to the CAUTION mode. The concept simply does not appear in the institutional governance literature even at this resolution.

The paper also confirms that even when governance practitioners reference runtime AI control, they default to binary framing. "Guardrails on AI" appears as a topic in two of 20 interviews; "Guardrails effectively prevent harms" appears as an evaluation criterion in two of 20 interviews. Both instances frame guardrails in binary terms: they either exist or they do not; they either prevent harms or they fail to. There is no graduated notion — no concept of AI participating under a formally restricted non-empty output scope. The binary mental model of runtime AI control is thus confirmed not only at the level of technical mechanisms (Shamsujjoha et al., 2025; Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024) but also at the level of practitioner discourse.

Notably, the paper does identify a graduated intuition at the policy instrument layer: governance practitioners recognise a spectrum of AI policy instruments ranging from legislation (high legal force, low administrative agility) to voluntary guidance documents (low force, high agility), with intermediate instruments between them. This confirms that practitioners already understand governance need not be all-or-nothing — but that intuition operates at the institutional layer. It has not been translated to the runtime architectural layer. The proposed architecture formalises at runtime what practitioners already practice informally at the institutional layer: not every situation requires the same governance intensity; intermediate environmental conditions call for intermediate operational restrictions.

Finally, the paper identifies knowledge and expertise resource limitations as the most severe governance constraint even in Canada — the first country to launch a national AI strategy, with dedicated institutions, federal directives, and a proposed AI Act. In low-resource environments such as small-scale coastal fisheries, these gaps are orders of magnitude more severe. This strengthens the case for architecturally embedded governance rather than reliance on institutional governance capacity that may not exist in the operational environment.

## 8. Stage-Gate — Development Governance, Not Operational Governance

**Leppinen et al. (2026)** present a four-stage, three-gate process for guiding AI solution development for preventive maintenance, using Robust Portfolio Modelling to screen candidate AI algorithms. The decision gates function as go/no-go checkpoints — a binary gate at each stage — conditioned on accumulated development evidence across six objectives and 17 criteria. The "must-meet"/"should-meet" distinction creates a hard/soft binary, not a three-level graduated model.

The notes are direct on the structural difference: "this paper's gates operate at the meta-level of AI development (deciding which AI to build), not at the operational level of AI deployment (deciding what AI may do in a given environmental state)." The gate function here is G(development\_evidence) → {go, no-go}, not G(S) → {0, 1} conditioned on environmental safety state. Once deployed, the AI solution operates without the gate framework. The stage-gate framework terminates at deployment; the proposed architecture begins there.

**Porter et al. (2025)** contribute INSYTE — a multi-dimensional classification framework for AI systems using eight dimensions (underspecification, adaptiveness, breadth, depth, environmental diversity, environmental dynamism, intervention independence, oversight independence), each rated 0–5, visualised as a radar chart. The separation of Dimension 7 (intervention independence) from Dimension 8 (oversight independence) independently validates the principle that governance should not be collapsed into a single dimension — supporting the architectural decision to separate G(S) from A\_AI(S) as distinct governance mechanisms. Dimension 7 classifies systems as decision support (Level 2: informs) or decision direction (Level 3: directs) — static classifications for a given system configuration.

But INSYTE is a classification framework, not a governance architecture. It provides a vocabulary for describing AI systems from the outside; it does not define how AI should be governed from the inside. It classifies the degree of human control; it does not define the mechanism by which AI participation is governed at runtime. As the notes observe: INSYTE acknowledges that "a system's instantiation of some dimensions may change between intended use and unintended use, as well as between pre-deployment and operation" — motivating runtime architectures that dynamically adjust governance, precisely what the proposed architecture provides.

## 9. Systematic Review Evidence: The Gap Is Universal

Four independent systematic reviews, covering different bodies of literature from different methodological perspectives, converge on the same finding.

**Ramos et al. (2024)** [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md) review 91 papers on collaborative intelligence for safety-critical industries (manufacturing, automotive, aviation, maritime, nuclear, rail) from 2014 to 2023. The governance mechanisms documented include supervisor synthesis using PCTL specifications, shared control arbitration, human intervention during RL training, simplex architectures, and dynamic safety zones. All are binary: AI operates or is overridden. The fuzzy three-level risk classification (Costanzo et al., reviewed by Ramos et al.) maps sensor inputs to high/medium/low risk levels controlling robot speed — the closest concept to tripartite classification in the 91-paper body. But it operates on a single continuous output parameter, not a discrete recommendation space. The notes confirm: "across 91 reviewed papers, all safety governance mechanisms are binary — AI operates or is blocked/overridden. No paper implements graduated governance where AI participates with restricted recommendation scope under intermediate risk." Level 2 advisory scope governance: no paper restricts AI output types based on safety state.

**Perez-Cerrolaza et al. (2024)** [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) survey AI safety techniques for safety-critical systems across automotive, avionics, railway, and industrial domains in an ACM Computing Surveys article of 40 pages and 294 references. The safety bag, safety envelope, and runtime monitor patterns universally implement binary control: AI outputs are either within the safety envelope (permitted) or outside it (blocked, safe state activated). The Class I/II/III taxonomy and the SIL/ASIL/DAL integrity levels classify design-time system integrity requirements, not runtime environmental states. The survey notes that excessive binary blocking can create "new system-level hazards" from false alarm cognitive overload — a direct motivation for the graduated approach the proposed architecture provides. No paper implements a mode where AI participates under restricted advisory scope. "All safety mechanisms described operate as binary switches: AI output is either within the safety envelope (permitted) or outside it (blocked → safe state)."

**Bengio et al. (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md) — the International AI Safety Report — review 11 Frontier AI Safety Frameworks from organisations responsible for the world's most capable AI systems. All 11 frameworks implement Level 1 governance (capability thresholds triggering deployment decisions). None implements state-conditioned advisory scope restriction. The report documents that "no combination of safeguards is perfectly reliable" and that "developers cannot yet provide robust, quantifiable assurances that AI systems will avoid specific harmful behaviours." Binary control is confirmed as the universal paradigm at the policy level.

**Newcomb & Ochoa (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md) review 46 peer-reviewed studies of formal methods for safety-critical machine learning across eight formal method categories. Every method surveyed operates with binary safety boundaries. The binary paradigm extends from applied engineering frameworks (Perez-Cerrolaza, Ramos) through policy governance (Bengio) into formal verification (Newcomb & Ochoa). This is four independent streams of evidence converging on the same architectural gap.

## 10. Why Baxi Does Not Fill the Gap

**Baxi (2026)** [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) is the structurally closest prior work and merits dedicated treatment. The Comprehension-Gated Agent Economy (CGAE) is an arXiv preprint presenting a formal architecture where AI agents' economic permissions are upper-bounded by a verified comprehension gate function derived from adversarial robustness audits. The gate function f(R) = T\_k maps a robustness vector R = (CC, ER, AS, IH) to discrete economic tiers via a weakest-link formulation: k = min(g₁(CC), g₂(ER), g₃(AS)), where each g\_i is a monotonically non-decreasing step function. Each tier T\_k defines a different permitted economic action set E ⊆ Σ with nested containment: E(T\_k) ⊂ E(T\_{k+1}). Three formal properties are proved: Theorem 3 (bounded economic exposure: E(A,t) ≤ B\_{f(R\_eff(A,t))}), Theorem 5 (incentive-compatible robustness investment), and Theorem 6 (monotonic safety scaling).

The structural parallel is precise. The gate function f(R) → T\_k mirrors the safety state classification f(E) → S. The permission sets E(T\_k) mirror A\_AI(S). The nested containment mirrors A\_AI(SAFE) ⊃ A\_AI(CAUTION) ⊃ A\_AI(UNSAFE) = ∅. The bounded exposure theorem mirrors the Safety Dominance Property. The weakest-link principle parallels worst-case environmental state determination.

Five differences prevent CGAE from filling the gap.

**(1) Conditioning variable.** CGAE gates on verified agent robustness properties — a static, offline-assessed characterisation of how trustworthy the AI is. The proposed architecture gates on classified environmental safety state — a dynamic, real-time characterisation of how dangerous the current conditions are. Baxi's R is agent-centric and assessed before deployment; f(E) is environment-centric and evaluated continuously at runtime. Baxi's governance answers "how much can this AI be trusted to do?" The proposed governance answers "what is the AI permitted to do given current environmental danger?" These are orthogonally different conditioning variables addressing orthogonally different safety questions.

**(2) No environmental classification function.** CGAE has no S = f(E). There is no function mapping observable environmental parameters to discrete safety states. CGAE does not classify operational environment; it classifies agent robustness. The proposed pipeline E → S = f(E) → (G(S), A\_AI(S)) → AI(E) depends on the environmental classification as the input to governance. This layer has no analogue in CGAE.

**(3) No runtime reclassification.** Because CGAE's conditioning variable is robustness — assessed offline via adversarial audit — the tier assignment is static between audits. Temporal decay (δ(Δt) = e^{-λΔt}) and stochastic re-auditing create some dynamism, but this is audit scheduling, not real-time environmental monitoring. When weather deteriorates in a maritime context, the proposed architecture reclassifies from SAFE to CAUTION immediately; CGAE has no mechanism for this.

**(4) Domain.** CGAE is designed for AI economic agency — trading, contracts, procurement, sub-agent spawning. It operates in the domain of financial risk, not physical safety-criticality. The governance consequences of tier violations are financial (budget ceilings B\_k). The proposed architecture operates in physical safety-critical contexts where governance failures have consequences for human safety.

**(5) Citation quality.** CGAE is an arXiv preprint (arXiv:2603.15639v2) by an independent researcher with alumni affiliation only. The DOI (10.48550/arXiv.2603.15639) is arXiv-assigned, not a journal or conference DOI — it does not indicate peer review. Theorem 3 has not been independently verified. The formal content is the strongest in the graduated governance literature, but the absence of peer review and empirical validation means CGAE cannot serve as a load-bearing gap claim or primary architectural reference.

CGAE and the proposed architecture are structurally parallel but orthogonally conditioned — complementary, not competing. A complete governance system would integrate both: AI permissions should depend on both verified AI robustness (CGAE's conditioning variable) and current environmental safety state (the proposed architecture's conditioning variable). Neither paper addresses the other's conditioning dimension.

## 11. The Gap — Stated Precisely

The gap has three components.

**Architectural gap.** No unified governance mechanism exists where environmental safety state simultaneously controls both AI participation and AI advisory recommendation scope in a formally defined architecture. The literature implements Level 1 governance (participation control) extensively, in forms ranging from safety shields (Könighofer et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) to human-AI safety filters (Bajcsy & Fisac, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) to guaranteed safe AI verifiers (Dalrymple et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md) to per-action runtime enforcement (Wang et al., 2026) [[notes]](../notes/AgentSpec-%20Customizable%20Runtime%20Enforcement%20for%20Safe%20and%20Reliable%20LLM%20Agents.md). The literature implements Level 2 governance (output scope restriction) in the form of guardrail taxonomies (Shamsujjoha et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md), guardrail systems (Chen et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md), and per-output gatekeepers (Liang et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md). No work implements both levels unified under a classified environmental safety state S ∈ {SAFE, CAUTION, UNSAFE} derived from a vector of observable environmental parameters E.

**Formal gap.** The closest existing formal safety properties are binary: the safety filter's Theorem 1 (never enter the failure set, Bajcsy & Fisac, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md), the shield's winning region guarantee (Könighofer et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md), the GS AI verifier's proof certificate (Dalrymple et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Towards%20Guaranteed%20Safe%20AI-%20A%20Framework%20for%20Ensuring%20Robust%20and%20Reliable%20AI%20Systems.md), the Tumato soundness guarantee (Vermaelen & Holvoet, 2025) [[notes]](../notes/umato%202.0%20%E2%80%94%20A%20Constraint-Based%20Planning%20Approach%20for%20Safe%20and%20Robust%20Robot%20Behavior.md), and AI Safety Principle I (Y ∩ Z = ∅, Chen et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md). The only graduated formal property in the reviewed literature is Baxi's Theorem 3 (bounded economic exposure), and this operates over robustness-conditioned economic permissions, not environment-conditioned physical safety governance. No Safety Dominance Property of the form AI(E) ⊆ A\_AI(S) has been defined for a graduated governance architecture conditioning both levels on classified environmental state in a physical safety-critical domain.

**Evaluation gap.** No study has compared graduated governance (CAUTION mode: G(S) = 1, A\_AI restricted) against binary governance (G(S) = 0) in a safety-critical physical domain. No user study has evaluated the CAUTION mode — whether restricted-scope AI advisory under intermediate conditions produces better human decision outcomes than full blocking. Könighofer et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) empirically demonstrate that intermediate-restrictiveness shields (ε = 0.03, 0.05) outperform both no restriction and maximum restriction, providing indirect empirical motivation. Liang et al. (2025) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md) document the accuracy-failure trade-off of binary pass/reject governance. But no study directly tests graduated governance with advisory scope restriction in a safety-critical decision support context.

The gap is confirmed independently by 91 papers (Ramos et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), 46 formal methods studies (Newcomb & Ochoa, 2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md), 11 international AI safety frameworks (Bengio et al., 2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), and the cross-domain survey (Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md).

## 13. One-Paragraph Examiner Response

The proposed architecture — a two-level governance pair (G(S), A\_AI(S)) unified under environmental safety state S = f(E) — is genuinely new. The safety shielding literature (Könighofer et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Shields%20for%20Safe%20Reinforcement%20Learning.md) and human-AI safety filter literature (Bajcsy & Fisac, 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Human%E2%80%93AI%20Safety-%20A%20Descendant%20of%20Generative%20AI%20and%20Control%20Systems%20Safety.md) provide the most formally rigorous Level 1 governance in the field, but both implement binary participation control — either the AI operates or it is overridden — with no intermediate mode where AI continues under restricted advisory scope. The guardrail literature (Shamsujjoha et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Liang et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Swiss%20Cheese%20Model%20for%20AI%20Safety-%20A%20Taxonomy%20and%20Reference%20Architecture%20for%20Multi-Layered%20Guardrails%20of%20Foundation%20Model%20Based%20Agents.md) [[notes]](../notes/SHIELDAGENT-%20Shielding%20Agents%20via%20Verifiable%20Safety%20Policy%20Reasoning.md) [[notes]](../notes/Safeguarded%20AI-Driven%20Semantic%20Communication-%20Design%20Principles%2C%20Architecture%2C%20and%20Challenges.md) comprehensively catalogues Level 2 output governance actions, but these are applied statically without conditioning on classified environmental state. Graduated systems in the adaptive autonomy literature (Flehmig et al., 2024; Gyllenhammar et al., 2025) [[notes]](../notes/Implementing%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20in%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20during%20Operation-%20Challenges%20and%20Extended%20Framework%20for%20a%20Quality%20Assurance%20Process.md) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Road%20to%20Safe%20Automated%20Driving%20Systems-%20A%20Review%20of%20Methods%20Providing%20Safety%20Evidence.md) classify operational levels but use the intermediate level to govern supervisory intensity or domain restriction, never AI advisory scope type. Four independent systematic reviews — 91 collaborative intelligence papers (Ramos et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Collaborative%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Industries-%20A%20Literature%20Review.md), 46 formal methods studies (Newcomb & Ochoa, 2026) [[notes]](../notes/Formal%20methods%20for%20safety-critical%20machine%20learning%3A%20a%20systematic%20literature%20review.md), 11 international AI safety frameworks (Bengio et al., 2026) [[notes]](../notes/International%20AI%20Safety%20Report%202026.md), and the cross-domain safety-critical AI survey (Perez-Cerrolaza et al., 2024) [[notes]](../notes/Artificial%20Intelligence%20for%20Safety-Critical%20Systems%20in%20Industrial%20and%20Transportation%20Domains-%20A%20Survey.md) — all confirm the same finding: binary governance is universal. The one structurally closest work (Baxi, 2026, preprint) [[notes]](../notes/The%20Comprehension-Gated%20Agent%20Economy-%20A%20Robustness-First%20Architecture%20for%20AI%20Economic%20Agency.md) proves graduated governance is formalisable but conditions it on verified agent robustness rather than dynamic environmental state, operating in an AI economic domain rather than a physical safety-critical one, and is not peer-reviewed. No prior work implements a unified architecture where the same classified environmental safety state simultaneously determines whether AI may participate at all and what types of recommendations AI is permitted to generate, with a formal Safety Dominance Property guaranteeing AI(E) ⊆ A\_AI(S) across all conditions.
